Cumings, “The Virtues”

Chapter 1 of Korea’s Place in the Sun, pp. 19-85
Bruce Cumings old Korea before 1860s rupture Confucianism mind-heart early Korea

Why Cumings Starts Here

Cumings opens his book by arguing that “modern” Korea cannot be understood without the older worlds that shaped Korean habits, emotions, politics, and social expectations. His target is a lazy historical view that treats Korea as merely a corridor between China and Japan. Instead, he insists that Korea absorbed outside influences and remade them in distinctly Korean ways.

Core Claim

Korea is not a passive recipient of civilization. It is a long historical formation with its own moral vocabulary, social structure, regional consciousness, political traditions, and inherited emotional world.

Two Big Streams

1. Confucian Virtue

Cumings treats Confucianism not as a dead doctrine but as an ordering system that shaped family hierarchy, education, self-cultivation, officeholding, and political legitimacy. He does not reduce it to obedience alone. He emphasizes both its conservative side and its capacity to authorize moral criticism, learning, and disciplined public life.

2. Native Mind-Heart

Alongside the Confucian stream, Cumings places a deeper and less fully articulated current of Korean belief and feeling: the sim, or mind-heart. This is where intuition, emotion, spirits, song, shamanic practice, bodily knowledge, and visceral attachment live. For him, Korea is not only rule and hierarchy; it is also affect, rhythm, landscape, and embodied feeling.

What To Watch For

  • He repeatedly refuses the idea that Korea was “Sinicized” in any simple or total way.
  • He frames Korean history as a process of Koreanizing foreign influence.
  • He keeps moral order and emotional life in tension rather than choosing one over the other.

Section Map

The chapter moves quickly across a long timeline. The point is not to memorize every dynasty detail, but to see how a moral and political world gets built.

Main Arc of the Chapter

  1. Origins and identity: Tan’gun, Paektu Mountain, regionalism, and the problem of national beginnings.
  2. State formation: the Three Kingdoms, Silla, and Koryŏ as stages in building Korean political order.
  3. Social transformation: hierarchy, family order, education, mobility, and the ethical centrality of filial piety.
  4. Court pathology: the Yŏngjo-Sado story as an extreme case of Confucian family-state logic.
  5. Limits of the old order: relatively weak commercial development and a late Chosŏn system that could not answer nineteenth-century pressure.

Why This Matters for the Course

This chapter gives the long historical background for the class’s literature side as much as its film side. Cumings is trying to explain how moral order, political culture, kinship, memory, and historical self-understanding were built before the modern rupture of the late nineteenth century.

Course Bridge

Use this chapter to track recurring questions that come back across the term: how Korean writers and filmmakers inherit older moral vocabularies, how family order becomes political order, and how modern Korea repeatedly argues with the past even when it claims to have escaped it.

Cumings also makes a more unsettling point: old Korea was not simply stable or harmonious. Its structures could generate strain, exclusion, and political disaster even while presenting themselves as morally complete.

Terms to Keep in Mind

Sim (mind-heart)

Not just “mind” in the rational sense. A chest-centered, affective, moral, bodily way of knowing.

Hyo (filial piety)

The core relation of father and son becomes the model for household, society, and state.

Tan’gun

More than a mythic founder. A recurring way Koreans narrate antiquity, legitimacy, and national continuity.

Koreanization

Cumings’s preferred direction of explanation: foreign influence enters Korea, but is transformed within Korean history rather than simply copied.

Questions for Class

Discussion Prompts

  • What does Cumings gain by pairing Confucian order with the more intuitive and shamanic world of the mind-heart?
  • Why is he so resistant to describing Korea as merely a bridge between China and Japan?
  • How does the Yŏngjo-Sado episode expose the dangers of collapsing family authority and political sovereignty into one structure?
  • Does the chapter describe a vanished moral world, or one whose fragments still organize modern Korean life?
