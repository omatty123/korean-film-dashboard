The Flower Girl

꽃파는 처녀
Dir. Choe Ik-gyu (최익규) / Scr. Pak Hak (박학) 1972 Color Revolutionary Drama DPRK Cinema Watch Film

Historical Context: Colonial Suffering and Revolutionary Art

The Flower Girl draws on an experience that was devastatingly real for millions of Koreans: life under Japanese colonial rule (1910-1945). Forced labor, land seizure, cultural suppression, and everyday violence defined the colonial period. The landlord class (often collaborating with Japanese authorities) extracted wealth from peasant families who had no legal recourse. The film's depiction of Kkotpun's suffering is not invented. It is a composite of what Korean families actually endured.

This matters because Western analysis of DPRK cinema tends to start and stop at "propaganda," as if labeling the work settles the question. It does not. The colonial wound was real. The poverty was real. The resistance was real. What makes The Flower Girl worth studying is not whether it serves the state (all national cinemas do, in different ways) but how it transforms lived historical experience into a narrative form that resonated across borders, from Pyongyang to Beijing to Prague.

The DPRK developed a distinctive film tradition grounded in Kim Il-sung's 1966 directive: "Our art should develop in a revolutionary way, reflecting the Socialist content with the national form." This was not simply an order to make propaganda. It was an insistence on cultural sovereignty, that Korean art should be neither Soviet imitation nor Western borrowing, but something rooted in Korean experience. The Korean Art Film Studio, founded in 1947 outside Pyongyang, became the center of this effort, eventually employing roughly 1,800 workers and producing up to 80 films per year at its peak. (Cinema of North Korea, Wikipedia)

Seed Theory and Juche Aesthetics

In 1973, one year after The Flower Girl's release, Kim Jong-il published On the Art of the Cinema, which codified DPRK filmmaking philosophy through the "seed theory" (종자론, chongjaron). Every work of art, Kim argued, must grow from a single ideological "seed," the core experience or truth that organizes all its elements. This is often reduced to "propaganda manual" in Western commentary. But the seed theory also reflects a serious (if restrictive) aesthetic position: that art must be unified around a central vision rather than assembled from competing impulses. (On the Art of the Cinema, Wikipedia)

Revolutionary Opera Origins

The Flower Girl began its life not as a film but as a stage play. According to DPRK official history, Kim Il-sung wrote the original script in the late 1920s while he was a student in Jilin, Manchuria. He reportedly staged the play in the Samsong school hall on the 13th anniversary of the October Revolution (November 1930). The story circulated among anti-Japanese guerrilla fighters during the 1930s as a piece of revolutionary agitprop, though independent verification of these claims is impossible. (CultureReady)

After decades in dormancy, the work was revived in the early 1970s under Kim Jong-il's supervision as part of a broader campaign to create a canon of revolutionary operas. The first of these, Sea of Blood (피바다), premiered at the Pyongyang Grand Theatre in July 1971. The Flower Girl followed as the second of what would become the "Five Great Revolutionary Operas." The opera premiered on November 30, 1972, in Pyongyang. (Korean revolutionary opera, Wikipedia)

Authorship and Legitimacy

The attribution of The Flower Girl to Kim Il-sung serves a specific political function. By tracing the work to the 1930s guerrilla period, the regime connects its cultural achievements directly to the anti-Japanese resistance, the founding myth of the DPRK. Chinese critics have questioned whether Kim Il-sung was the sole author, suggesting that other North Korean writers may have contributed. But the authorship claim is less about literary credit than about political genealogy. It places the Kim family at the origin point of Korean revolutionary culture itself. (The Flower Girl, Wikipedia)

The Film

Plot

The story is set in the 1930s under Japanese colonial occupation. Kkotpun (꽃분), a young woman, sells flowers on the street to support her ailing mother and her younger sister Sunhui, who was blinded by the landlord's wife throwing lye in her face. The landlord killed Kkotpun's father. Her brother, a resistance fighter, has been imprisoned by the Japanese. The family's suffering is total: economic exploitation, physical violence, and the crushing weight of colonial authority. (IMDb)

Kkotpun endures her degradation with patience, selling flowers and caring for her family. The narrative arc bends toward liberation when her brother escapes from prison and returns as a revolutionary guerrilla. The film ends with the family joining the anti-Japanese resistance, transforming from passive victims into active participants in their own liberation. This trajectory, from suffering under oppression to awakening through revolutionary consciousness, is the fundamental narrative template of all DPRK revolutionary art. (Cinema Escapist)

Production and Cast

The film was directed by Choe Ik-gyu with a screenplay by Pak Hak. It was produced by the Paekdu-san Group and shot in color. The lead role of Kkotpun was performed by Hong Yong-hee (홍영희), whose portrayal made her the most famous actress in North Korean history. She was later depicted on the DPRK's one-won banknote in her role as the flower girl, an honor she held until the currency was made obsolete by hyperinflation in 2009. A statue at the Pyongyang Film Studio depicts Kim Il-sung standing among the film's cast and crew. (Cinema Escapist / Wikipedia)

"North Korea's Gone with the Wind."

James Bell, British Film Institute, describing The Flower Girl

International Reception

The Flower Girl was the first North Korean film to win an international award, receiving a special prize and special medal at the 18th Karlovy Vary International Film Festival in Czechoslovakia in 1972. It remained the only DPRK film to achieve international recognition until the 1980s. The film screened widely in Eastern Bloc countries and throughout Asia. (The Flower Girl, Wikipedia)

Its most extraordinary reception came in China. Introduced to Chinese cinemas on September 9, 1972, during the final years of the Cultural Revolution, the film became a sensation. Its themes of proletarian suffering and revolutionary awakening resonated with Chinese audiences starved of new cultural material (most Chinese films and operas had been banned). Demand was so high that some theaters adopted 24-hour screening cycles. Nobel Prize laureate Mo Yan later described seeing the film in 1973 as "an event that I've never forgotten and that remains tied to today's political and social life." (Cinema Escapist / Wikipedia)

DPRK Cinema in Context

The Flower Girl emerged from a film tradition that took cinema seriously as a national art form. The DPRK invested heavily in film production from the state's founding, treating it not as commercial entertainment but as cultural infrastructure, comparable to how other nations fund national theaters, opera companies, or public broadcasting. The Korean Art Film Studio's million-square-meter campus reflects this commitment. (Cinema of North Korea, Wikipedia)

DPRK cinema explores recurring themes rooted in Korean historical experience: resistance to foreign occupation, the Korean War, socialist construction, and daily life. Anti-Japanese occupation films draw on the same colonial trauma that The Flower Girl depicts. War films reflect a conflict that killed millions and remains technically unresolved. Domestic dramas explore social ideals. What unites them is the juche principle of cultural self-reliance: Korean stories told in Korean forms. (Cinema of North Korea, Wikipedia)

The Korean Art Film Studio

The DPRK's main production facility, the Korean Art Film Studio, sits outside Pyongyang on a campus exceeding one million square meters. Founded in 1947, it is run by the Ministry of Culture and employs approximately 1,800 people. The studio contains elaborate sets designed to replicate colonial-era Korea, Manchuria in the 1930s, imperial Japan, and a generic "Western country." Visitors to the studio are shown the statue of Kim Il-sung among the cast of The Flower Girl, reinforcing the film's status as the industry's founding achievement. (Koryo Tours / Wikipedia)

Film Analysis and Course Connections

The Flower Girl's emotional power comes from the specificity of Kkotpun's suffering. She is not an abstraction. She is a young woman selling flowers because the landlord killed her father, blinded her sister, and the colonial system offers no recourse. Chinese audiences during the Cultural Revolution did not watch this film on 24-hour screening loops because they were ordered to. They recognized in Kkotpun's experience something that connected to their own histories of exploitation and dispossession.

The film's narrative arc moves from suffering to resistance. Kkotpun endures, and then her brother returns as a revolutionary. The family joins the struggle. This is a specific storytelling choice, but it is not unique to North Korean cinema. Sopyonje tells a story of cultural endurance. Taebaek Mountains narrates ideological awakening. A Single Spark dramatizes a labor activist's sacrifice. Every film in this course channels historical experience toward a particular interpretation. The question worth asking is not whether The Flower Girl does this (it does, as do they all) but what specific historical truths it illuminates and what interpretive choices it makes.

Connection to Course Themes

  • Colonial resistance narratives. Both Koreas use the anti-Japanese resistance as a source of national identity. The Flower Girl centers the guerrilla tradition. South Korean films like Taebaek Mountains and Assassination (2015) center different strands of the resistance. Comparing these narratives reveals how the same historical period generates competing national stories.
  • Film as cultural memory. The Flower Girl preserves a specific experience of colonial-era Korea: rural poverty, landlord exploitation, gendered suffering. Whether or not one accepts the film's political conclusions, the social world it depicts reflects documented historical conditions.
  • Cross-border resonance. The Flower Girl's success in China, Eastern Europe, and across Asia shows that a film rooted in Korean colonial experience can speak to audiences whose own histories include exploitation and resistance. Its emotional core transcended its production context.
  • National cinema traditions. Studying The Flower Girl alongside South Korean films from the same era (like Aimless Bullet, banned by Park Chung-hee's government) reveals that both Korean states shaped their film industries according to political priorities. The forms differed. The impulse to make cinema serve national purposes did not.

The opera version of The Flower Girl has been performed over 1,400 times in North Korea and in more than 40 countries, including France, Italy, Germany, Algeria, and Japan. As of 2008, it remained one of the most-performed works in the DPRK's cultural repertoire. The film and the opera together form a single cultural monument: the origin story that North Korea tells about itself. (CultureReady)

Key Concepts

Juche Art 주체예술
Art governed by the juche (self-reliance) ideology. Kim Il-sung's 1966 directive demanded art that reflects "socialist content with the national form." All DPRK cultural production must serve the revolution while remaining distinctly Korean.
Revolutionary Opera 혁명가극
A genre of opera unique to North Korea, combining music, dance, and drama to dramatize revolutionary themes. Characterized by melodramatic style, realistic sets, dream sequences, and glorification of the Kim family and working people.
Seed Theory 종자론
From Kim Jong-il's On the Art of the Cinema (1973). Every work of art must be organized around a single ideological "seed," the synthesis of subject matter and propaganda message. The seed is the nucleus from which all elements of the work grow.
Socialist Realism
Soviet-originated artistic doctrine demanding that art depict reality in its "revolutionary development." The DPRK adopted and modified this into juche realism, adding Korean nationalist elements to the Soviet template.
Chollywood
Informal name for the DPRK film industry. A portmanteau of "chollima" (천리마, a mythical winged horse symbolizing rapid socialist progress) and "Hollywood."

The Five Revolutionary Operas

  • 1. Sea of Blood (피바다, 1971). The first revolutionary opera. A woman's family is destroyed by Japanese occupiers, leading her to join the communist resistance. Performed over 1,500 times.
  • 2. The Flower Girl (꽃파는 처녀, 1972). A young flower seller endures colonial oppression until her brother returns from prison as a revolutionary. Adapted into the landmark 1972 film.
  • 3. A True Daughter of the Party (당의 참된 딸, 1971). A woman demonstrates unwavering loyalty to the Party during wartime hardship.
  • 4. Tell O' the Forest! (밀림아 이야기하라, 1972). Choe Byong-hung, a revolutionary who pretends to serve the Japanese, endures the hatred of his own villagers, who find his deception too convincing.
  • 5. The Song of Mount Kumgang (금강산의 노래, 1973). The final canonical opera, completing the cycle of "immortal classics."

Discussion Questions

  1. The Flower Girl depicts real conditions of colonial Korea: landlord exploitation, Japanese imprisonment, gendered poverty. How does Kkotpun's story compare to the colonial experiences depicted in Sopyonje or the readings by Na Hyesok and Kang Kyeong-ae?
  2. Chinese audiences in the 1970s watched this film obsessively. What in the story connected with their own historical experience? What does cross-border resonance tell us about shared histories of exploitation in East Asia?
  3. Compare the role of cinema in the DPRK with the role of cinema in South Korea under Park Chung-hee (who banned Aimless Bullet). Both states shaped their film industries. What are the similarities and differences?
  4. Hong Yong-hee appeared on the DPRK's one-won banknote in her role as Kkotpun. What does it mean when a fictional character becomes a national symbol? Can you think of parallels in other countries?
  5. The film's authorship is attributed to Kim Il-sung in the 1930s. What function does this origin story serve? How do other nations construct similar founding narratives around cultural works?
  6. How does The Flower Girl's narrative structure (suffering followed by revolutionary awakening) compare to narrative patterns in South Korean films like A Single Spark or Taebaek Mountains?
