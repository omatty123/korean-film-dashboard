개벽 · Opening of a New World
Dir. Im Kwontaek (임권택) 1991 146 min Drama / Historical Epic
Watch Film

Historical Context: The Donghak Movement

In 1860, a failed scholar named Choe Che-u (also romanized Choe Je-u, 최제우) founded a new religious and philosophical movement called Donghak (동학, "Eastern Learning"). Alarmed by the spread of Catholicism (known in Korea as Seohak, "Western Learning") and the Anglo-French occupation of Beijing, Choe synthesized elements from Confucianism, Buddhism, Taoism, and Korean shamanism into a doctrine that proclaimed all human beings equal before heaven. His core teaching, Innaech'on (人乃天, "humans are heaven"), radically challenged the rigid class hierarchies of the Chosŏn dynasty. (Britannica)

Donghak spread rapidly among impoverished peasants and displaced farmers in the southern provinces. The religion gave them both spiritual solace and a vocabulary for political resistance. The Chosŏn court, viewing the movement as seditious, arrested Choe Che-u and executed him in 1864. His death made him a martyr. (Britannica)

The Second Patriarch: Choe Si-hyŏng

After the founder's execution, Choe Si-hyŏng (최시형, 1827-1898) took over leadership. Born into poverty in Kyŏngju, orphaned young, he worked as a farmhand and paper mill laborer before encountering Donghak in 1861. As the second patriarch, he reorganized the movement through underground networks, published the first two Donghak scriptures in 1880-81, and expanded the doctrine: people must not only recognize their equality before heaven, they must "serve Man as they serve heaven." In 1892 he mobilized tens of thousands for a peaceful demonstration calling for the founder's posthumous exoneration and administrative reform. (Britannica)

This figure is the central protagonist of Fly High Run Far. The film dramatizes his struggle to preserve and propagate Donghak while evading the court's relentless persecution.

The 1894 Donghak Peasant Revolution

By the early 1890s, conditions in southern Korea had become unbearable. Government corruption was systemic: officials purchased their posts and then squeezed peasants to recoup the investment. The yangban (aristocratic) class had expanded dramatically, with many refusing manual labor even as they fell into poverty, shifting the tax burden onto farmers. Foreign encroachment, especially from Japan and Western powers, added to the crisis. (New World Encyclopedia)

The First Uprising (January-June 1894)

In January 1894, peasants led by Chŏn Pong-jun (전봉준, 1854-1895), a charismatic local teacher nicknamed the "Green Bean General" for his small stature, rose against the corrupt magistrate of Kobu county. After initial victories, the peasant army defeated government forces at the Battle of Hwangt'ohyŏn in May 1894 and occupied Chŏnju (Jeonju) Palace. The rebels demanded institutional reform and the expulsion of foreign influences. (New World Encyclopedia)

The Chŏnju Treaty (June 1894)

The peasant army and the government negotiated a truce. Peasants agreed to disarm in exchange for reform promises. This brief armistice allowed the peasants to establish local self-governance offices (chipkangso) across the Cholla provinces. However, the pause proved fatal to the movement's military momentum.

Foreign Intervention and the Second Uprising

Threatened by the rebellion, King Kojong requested Chinese military assistance. Japan, citing treaty provisions, dispatched its own troops uninvited. With both powers' armies on Korean soil, the Sino-Japanese War erupted in July 1894. Korea, the nominal reason for both countries' intervention, became a battlefield for imperial competition. (Britannica)

When the pro-Japanese government in Seoul suppressed reform, Chŏn Pong-jun launched a second uprising in October 1894. At the Battle of Ugŭmch'i in November, the peasant army faced Japanese troops armed with cannons and modern rifles. The peasants, carrying bamboo spears, swords, and flintlock muskets, suffered devastating losses. Chŏn Pong-jun was captured in December 1894 and executed in 1895. Choe Si-hyŏng evaded capture until 1898, when he too was arrested and executed in Seoul. (New World Encyclopedia)

Why This Matters

The Donghak Peasant Revolution occupies a foundational place in Korean national consciousness. It was the first large-scale popular movement demanding equality, human rights, and national sovereignty. The revolution directly triggered the First Sino-Japanese War (1894-95), which ended Chinese suzerainty over Korea and opened the door to Japanese colonial domination. Many later participants in anti-Japanese resistance, including the independence leader Kim Ku, had roots in the Donghak movement. (New World Encyclopedia)

Director Profile: Im Kwontaek

Im Kwontaek (임권택, born May 2, 1936, Changsŏng, South Chŏlla Province) is widely called "the father of Korean cinema." Over a career spanning more than five decades, he directed over 102 films, making him one of the most prolific directors in world cinema history. (Britannica)

Im grew up in Kwangju during the Japanese occupation and the Korean War. After his father's death, he dropped out of school and drifted to Pusan, then Seoul, where director Chŏng Ch'ang-hwa offered him work as a production assistant in 1956. He made his directorial debut in 1962 with Farewell to the Duman River. His first decade produced roughly fifty genre films (action, melodrama, war pictures) made quickly for commercial survival. (Britannica)

The Turn Toward Korean Identity

Beginning in the late 1970s, Im underwent a creative transformation. He resolved to make films that were "uniquely Korean," exploring the country's history, spiritual traditions, and cultural identity. The breakthrough came with Mandala (1981), a meditation on Buddhist monasticism. From that point forward, each film became an excavation of Korean experience.

Selected Filmography

  • Mandala (1981). Two Buddhist monks on contrasting spiritual paths.
  • The Genealogy (Chokpo, 1978). Japanese occupation and Korean identity.
  • Fly High Run Far (Gaebyeok, 1991). The Donghak movement and Choe Si-hyŏng.
  • Sopyonje (1993). Pansori tradition and cultural loss. Most honored Korean film of its era, with 27 domestic and 3 international prizes.
  • T'aebaek Mountains (1994). Korean War and ideological partition.
  • Ch'unhyang (2000). Classical Korean love story through pansori.
  • Chihwasŏn (Painted Fire, 2002). 19th-century painter Jang Sŭng-ŭp. Won Best Director at Cannes, the first Korean director to receive the honor.

Im was a founding figure of the Korean New Wave alongside directors Park Kwang-su and Jang Sŏn-u. His international recognition helped open global audiences to Korean cinema, paving the way for later directors such as Bong Joon-ho and Park Chan-wook. He received the Akira Kurosawa Award from the San Francisco International Film Festival (1998) and the French Legion of Honour (2007). (Britannica)

Film Analysis

Plot and Structure

Fly High Run Far focuses on Choe Si-hyŏng, the second patriarch of the Donghak movement, rather than on the more commonly depicted military rebellion of 1894. The screenplay, by the philosopher Kim Yong-ok (김용옥), treats the Donghak story as a spiritual epic: it charts how a persecuted religious leader preserves a vision of human equality while the state hunts him across mountains and provinces. After the founder's execution, Choe inherits a scattered, underground movement and must rebuild it. As the religion grows, so does the court's determination to crush it. The film follows Choe's flight into the T'aebaek Mountains, his separation from his family, and his eventual capture. (KOFIC)

Title: "Gaebyeok" (개벽)

The Korean title carries more weight than the English one. Gaebyeok (개벽) means "the opening of a new world" or "great transformation." In Donghak theology, gaebyeok refers to a cosmic renewal: the old, corrupt order dissolving to make way for a world where all humans are treated as heaven. It is not simply revolution in the political sense but a total spiritual and social rebirth.

Themes

Spiritual resistance as political resistance. The film frames Donghak not primarily as a protest movement but as a way of being in the world. Choe Si-hyŏng's insistence that "all humans are heaven" is itself an act of defiance against a feudal system built on rigid hierarchy.

The cost of belief. Im Kwontaek's camera lingers on the physical and emotional toll of Choe's fugitive existence. Separated from family, hunted by soldiers, moving between safe houses and mountain hermitages, the patriarch's devotion consumes his entire life.

Peasant Korea. The film's visual language draws on the landscape of rural Chŏlla and the T'aebaek range. Im uses the natural world not as backdrop but as a character: the mountains shelter the faithful, the fields feed them, and the seasons mark the passage of persecution.

Connection to Course Themes

  • Roots of Korean nationalism. Donghak was among the earliest indigenous movements to articulate Korean identity in opposition to both the Chinese world order and Western imperialism.
  • Peasant resistance and class. The yangban system's collapse and the peasant demand for equality anticipate every subsequent Korean democratic movement.
  • Origins of colonialism. The Donghak uprising directly triggered the Sino-Japanese War, which ended Chinese influence in Korea and began Japan's path to colonial annexation (1910).
  • Minjung consciousness. The 1980s South Korean democracy movement explicitly claimed the Donghak rebels as predecessors. The minjung ("people's") movement saw itself as inheriting the peasants' unfinished struggle for equality and self-determination. (New World Encyclopedia)

Critical Reception

The film won Best Director and Best Actor at the 1991 Chunsa Film Festival and was selected for the Grand Bell Awards. It was also featured in the Korean Cinema Retrospective at the 2013 Busan International Film Festival. The critic Jonathan Rosenbaum called it "a beautiful and powerful spiritual epic." Despite its quality, the film remains under-discussed outside Korea, partly because it requires familiarity with the Donghak movement and late Chosŏn history to fully appreciate. (KOFIC)

Key Historical Figures

Choe Che-u (Choe Je-u)
최제우 · 1824-1864
Founder of Donghak
Son of a poor village scholar. Founded Donghak in 1860 after a claimed divine revelation. Synthesized Confucian, Buddhist, Taoist, and shamanistic elements into a doctrine of universal equality. Executed by the Chosŏn court in 1864 for fomenting rebellion. (Britannica)
Choe Si-hyŏng
최시형 · 1827-1898
Second Patriarch of Donghak (film's protagonist)
Orphaned farmhand who joined Donghak in 1861. Published the first Donghak scriptures, expanded the doctrine to "serve Man as heaven." Led tens of thousands in peaceful demonstrations. Evaded capture for decades before his execution in 1898. (Britannica)
Chŏn Pong-jun (Jeon Bong-jun)
전봉준 · 1854-1895
Military leader of the 1894 uprising
Known as the "Green Bean General" for his small stature. Led the peasant army to early victories at Kobu and Hwangt'ohyŏn. Defeated at Ugŭmch'i by Japanese firepower. Captured and executed in 1895. (KDemocracy)
Son Pyŏng-hŭi
손병희 · 1861-1922
Third Patriarch, renamed Donghak to Ch'ŏndogyo
Reorganized the movement as the religion Ch'ŏndogyo ("Religion of the Heavenly Way"). Later became one of the 33 signatories of Korea's March 1st Independence Declaration in 1919.

Key Terms

Donghak 동학
"Eastern Learning." A syncretic Korean religious and philosophical movement founded in 1860 as a counter to "Western Learning" (Christianity). Blended Confucian, Buddhist, Taoist, and shamanistic elements.
Innaech'ŏn 인내천 (人乃天)
"Humans are heaven." The core Donghak doctrine asserting that all people are divine and equal. Directly challenged the Chosŏn class system.
Gaebyeok 개벽
"Opening of a new world." In Donghak theology, a cosmic transformation where the corrupt old order dissolves and a new era of equality begins. The film's Korean title.
Yangban 양반
The aristocratic ruling class of the Chosŏn dynasty. By the 19th century, the class had expanded enormously while peasant burdens grew, fueling resentment.
Chipkangso 집강소
Local self-governance offices established by Donghak peasants in Chŏlla province after the Chŏnju Treaty (1894). An experiment in grassroots democracy.
Ch'ŏndogyo 천도교
"Religion of the Heavenly Way." The successor religion to Donghak, renamed by the third patriarch Son Pyŏng-hŭi. Still practiced in both Koreas today.
Minjung 민중
"The people" or "the masses." A concept central to South Korea's 1970s-80s democracy movement, which traced its lineage directly to the Donghak rebels.

Discussion Questions

  1. The film focuses on Choe Si-hyŏng (the religious leader) rather than Chŏn Pong-jun (the military leader). What does this choice tell us about Im Kwontaek's understanding of the Donghak movement?
  2. How does the concept of innaech'ŏn ("humans are heaven") challenge both the Chosŏn class system and the incoming Western ideologies?
  3. The 1894 uprising was crushed by a combination of Korean government forces and Japanese troops. How does foreign intervention transform a domestic reform movement into a nationalist one?
  4. South Korea's democracy activists in the 1980s explicitly claimed the Donghak rebels as ancestors. Is this a legitimate historical connection or a political appropriation?
  5. The Korean title Gaebyeok means "opening of a new world." How does this differ from Western concepts of revolution? What does it reveal about the Donghak movement's self-understanding?
  6. Compare Im Kwontaek's treatment of traditional Korean culture here with his approach in Sopyonje (1993). What connects these two films?
