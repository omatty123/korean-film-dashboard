Deep Dive: Documentary

A State of Mind

마음의 상태
Daniel Gordon, 2004 93 minutes Documentary United Kingdom / DPRK

Historical Context

North Korea in 2003: The Hermit Kingdom on Camera

When Daniel Gordon's crew arrived in Pyongyang in February 2003, the Democratic People's Republic of Korea was one of the most isolated societies on earth. The country had endured a devastating famine throughout the 1990s that killed an estimated hundreds of thousands of people. Kim Jong-il had consolidated power following his father Kim Il-sung's death in 1994, maintaining a personality cult of extraordinary intensity.[1]

Western cameras had almost never been granted access to ordinary North Korean homes. Gordon and producer Nicholas Bonner, who co-founded Koryo Tours after first visiting the DPRK in 1993, leveraged years of relationship-building to secure something unprecedented: permission to follow two families in their daily lives for eight months.[2]

The result is not a political expose or a piece of propaganda. It is something rarer and more valuable: an observational documentary that lets viewers draw their own conclusions about a society built on total devotion to a single ruling family.

The Mass Games

Arirang: The Largest Human Performance on Earth

The Mass Games (formally the Arirang Festival) are a massive gymnastic and artistic performance held at the 150,000-seat Rungrado 1st of May Stadium in Pyongyang. Over 100,000 participants perform synchronized gymnastics, dance, and acrobatics in a 90-minute spectacle that blends athletic precision with political narrative.[3]

The name "Arirang" comes from a traditional Korean folk song that has served as a symbol of Korean identity for centuries. During the Japanese colonial occupation (1910-1945), when Korean language and culture were systematically suppressed, "Arirang" functioned as an expression of cultural resistance. UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list (South Korea in 2012, North Korea in 2014).[4]

The Mass Games were held annually from 2002 to 2013, then intermittently. The Arirang performance entered the Guinness Book of World Records in August 2007 for the largest gymnastic display on earth, with 109,000 participants.[3] The performances combine a massive human pixel screen (thousands of schoolchildren flipping colored cards in the stands to create images) with floor routines that tell the story of Korean history through the lens of the Workers' Party.

What the Film Shows

The film follows two young gymnasts preparing for the 2003 Mass Games. Pak Hyon-sun, age 13, is an only child. Kim Song-yun, age 11, comes from a family with three girls. Pak is the more experienced of the two; Kim learns gymnastic moves from her. Their relationship is one of mutual dependence and genuine friendship.[2]

Gordon's camera captures months of grueling practice sessions, moments of frustration and pride, family dinners, and school routines. The girls train relentlessly, not primarily for personal glory but because participation in the Mass Games is understood as a duty to the nation and its leader. The film climaxes with a striking montage drawn from the actual 40-minute performance.[5]

Director and Production

Daniel Gordon and the DPRK Trilogy

Daniel Gordon (born 1972, Manchester) is a British documentary filmmaker who previously worked for Sky Sports and Chrysalis before founding VeryMuchSo Productions in Sheffield in 2001.[6] He is best known for three documentaries made in North Korea, produced in collaboration with Nicholas Bonner of Koryo Tours:

  • The Game of Their Lives (2002): the story of the 1966 North Korean World Cup team that shocked Italy. Won a Royal Television Society award.
  • A State of Mind (2004): this film, following two gymnasts preparing for the Mass Games.
  • Crossing the Line (2006): the story of James Joseph Dresnok, an American soldier who defected to North Korea in 1962. Premiered at the Sundance Film Festival.

Gordon later directed the double BAFTA-winning Hillsborough (2016) about the 1989 stadium disaster, and The Australian Dream (2019), which won the AACTA Award for Best Feature Documentary.[6]

How They Got In

Access was made possible through Bonner's long-standing relationship with the DPRK. After visiting North Korea on a study trip from China in 1993, Bonner and colleague Joshua Green established Koryo Tours at the request of a North Korean contact, becoming one of the first Western travel operators in the country.[7]

Working with a North Korean producer identified only as Ryom, the crew was accompanied by guides and translators throughout production. Crucially, according to Gordon, the guides neither interfered with filming nor sought to censor the material. The footage was not screened by North Korean authorities before the crew left the country, and the DPRK had no editorial input during post-production.[1]

This level of access was and remains extraordinary. Filming began in February 2003, and the crew returned multiple times over eight months. They were allowed into the families' homes, workplaces, and schools. The question of how much the families' behavior was shaped by the presence of cameras (and state minders) is one the film leaves for viewers to consider.

Daily Life in Pyongyang

A Window, Not a Mirror

The documentary reveals details of daily Pyongyang life that were virtually unknown to Western audiences in 2004. The families live in modest but functional apartments. They eat together. The children attend school, study hard, and practice gymnastics for hours. The parents work at state-assigned jobs. Electricity is intermittent. Consumer goods are sparse.

The families' devotion to Kim Jong-il appears genuine and total. Portraits of the Great Leader and Dear Leader hang in every home. The adults speak of the leadership with reverence that Western viewers may find unsettling, though the film refuses to editorialize. This is the central tension of the documentary: it shows people who appear to be sincere believers in a system that, from outside, looks like an elaborate apparatus of control.

It is important to note that the families shown are Pyongyang residents, meaning they almost certainly belong to the "core" class in North Korea's songbun system. Life in the capital is materially better than in the provinces, particularly the rural northeast. The film is a portrait of privileged North Korean life, not average North Korean life.[8]

Critical Reception

Western Response

The film premiered at the Tribeca Film Festival in New York in June 2004 and was broadcast on BBC4 and ARTE the same month. It opened theatrically in the United States on August 10, 2004, and screened in Seoul later that month. It won two awards at the Pyongyang International Film Festival in 2004 and showed at eleven additional film festivals worldwide.[1]

The Los Angeles Times described it as "93 unblinking minutes of insight into the lives and minds of the people living in North Korea."[9] Variety called it "admirably non-judgmental" and praised it for bringing "a refreshing balance to the usual blind vilification of the country."[5] The film holds a 7.7 rating on IMDb.[10]

Critics consistently noted the film's refusal to tell viewers what to think. Some praised this restraint as a mark of documentary integrity. Others wondered whether the absence of editorial commentary risked normalizing a totalitarian state. Both readings are defensible, which is part of what makes the film valuable for classroom use.

Key Concepts

Juche (Self-Reliance)
The state ideology of the DPRK, developed by Kim Il-sung in the mid-1950s. Built on three pillars: political independence (jaju), economic independence (jarip), and military independence (jawi). Functions as a quasi-religious framework justifying the Kim dynasty's absolute rule. [Britannica]
Mass Games (Arirang Festival)
Massive choreographed performances involving over 100,000 participants. Held at Rungrado 1st of May Stadium (capacity 150,000) in Pyongyang. Combine gymnastics, dance, and a human pixel display to narrate DPRK history. Entered the Guinness World Records in 2007. [Wikipedia]
Songbun (Social Classification)
North Korea's hereditary caste system, classifying all citizens into three groups based on perceived political loyalty: core (about 25%), wavering (about 55%), and hostile (about 20%). Assigned at birth based on family background going back three generations. Determines access to education, housing, food, and employment. [HRW]
Personality Cult
The system of mandatory reverence for the Kim family. Portraits of Kim Il-sung and Kim Jong-il hang in every home, school, and workplace. Citizens wear lapel pins bearing the leaders' images. The film captures how thoroughly this devotion permeates daily life.
Arirang (Folk Song)
A traditional Korean folk song of unknown origin, possibly dating to the Goryeo period (935-1392). Became a symbol of Korean cultural resistance during Japanese colonial rule. Recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage. Its themes of separation and longing resonate with the divided peninsula. [Britannica]

Discussion Questions

1. The film does not use narration or expert commentary. How does this approach shape your interpretation of what you see? Is the absence of editorial framing a form of objectivity, or does it risk presenting the regime's self-image without challenge?
2. The families in the documentary are Pyongyang residents, almost certainly members of the "core" songbun class. How should we account for this selection bias when drawing conclusions about North Korean society?
3. The girls express what appears to be genuine devotion to Kim Jong-il. How do you distinguish sincere belief from performed loyalty in a surveillance state? Does the distinction matter?
4. Compare the Mass Games as depicted here with other forms of state-directed mass performance (Olympic opening ceremonies, Soviet parades). What is distinctive about the DPRK version?
5. Gordon was accompanied by state minders throughout filming. How does this fact affect the evidentiary value of the documentary? What can we learn from what is shown, and what might we infer from what is absent?
