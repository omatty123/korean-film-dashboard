Historical Context: The Tragedy of Crown Prince Sado

On a sweltering day in the intercalary fifth month of 1762, King Yeongjo of Joseon ordered his 27-year-old son, Crown Prince Sado, to climb into a wooden rice chest (뒤주, dwiju). The chest measured roughly 1.3 meters square. For eight days, Sado pounded on the walls and screamed for release. On the eighth night, his cries fell silent. He died of dehydration and heat exposure, locked in a grain container on the orders of his own father. (New World Encyclopedia)

This event is one of the most studied episodes in Korean history, and it is the central subject of Lee Joon-ik's 2015 film. The incident was not a sudden eruption of royal rage. It was the culmination of decades of tension between a reformist king and a troubled heir, set against a court system in which factional politics turned every personal failure into a political crisis. (Britannica)

The Father: King Yeongjo (1694-1776)

Yeongjo was the 21st monarch of the Joseon dynasty and its longest-reigning king, ruling for 52 years from 1724 to 1776. He was the second son of King Sukjong and a concubine of low birth, a fact that shadowed his legitimacy throughout his reign. Yeongjo was a deeply Confucian ruler, said to have possessed a greater knowledge of the classics than his own officials. He implemented significant reforms: halving the military service tax, rationalizing state revenue accounting, and upgrading the social status of children born to commoner families. (Britannica / New World Encyclopedia)

His defining political project was t'angp'yŏng (蕩平, "grand harmony"), a deliberate policy of balancing rival factions at court. After inheriting a government torn apart by decades of factional purges under his predecessors, Yeongjo pursued calculated pardons, strategic ministerial reshuffles, and Confucian exhortations against partisan loyalty. The policy had real limitations, but it represented a serious attempt to subordinate factional interest to royal authority. (Dark Side of Seoul)

The Son: Crown Prince Sado (1735-1762)

Sado was born on February 13, 1735, as the second son of King Yeongjo. His older half-brother, Crown Prince Hyojang, had already died, making Sado the sole heir apparent from an early age. The weight of succession fell on him before he could walk. According to Lady Hyegyeong's memoirs, the young prince showed early promise but grew increasingly troubled as he matured. His father's exacting standards and frequent rebukes created a dynamic of fear and resentment. (Korea History Insights)

A Question of Diagnosis

Modern historians and psychologists have debated the nature of Sado's condition. Lady Hyegyeong's memoirs describe violent episodes, including attacks on palace servants and members of his own household. Some scholars interpret these accounts as evidence of severe mental illness, possibly triggered by the relentless pressure of his father's expectations and the toxic environment of court factionalism. Others emphasize the political dimensions: Sado was aligned with the Soron faction, while Yeongjo leaned toward the Noron, and the prince's "madness" may have been amplified or even manufactured by courtiers who wanted him removed from the succession. The film takes a nuanced approach, presenting Sado as both genuinely troubled and politically destroyed. (Cambridge University Press)

The Method of Execution

Yeongjo could not simply execute his son. Under Joseon law, a royal body could not be defiled by the executioner's blade. More critically, if Sado were formally condemned as a traitor, the principle of communal punishment (연좌제, yŏnjwaje) would extend to his wife and his son, the young Prince Jeongjo, who was the dynasty's only remaining direct male heir. The rice chest was a legal workaround: Sado would die, but not by execution, preserving the succession. Yeongjo posthumously granted his son the title "Prince Sado" (思悼世子), meaning "Prince of Sorrow" or "Prince Thought of with Grief." (Mad Monarchs)

Joseon Court Politics: Noron vs. Soron

The Sado tragedy cannot be understood apart from the factional system that defined Joseon political life. By the 18th century, the court was dominated by two rival blocs that had emerged from a split in the Westerner (서인, Sŏin) faction during the late 17th century. The Noron (老論, "Old Doctrine") followed the intellectual lineage of Song Si-yŏl, while the Soron (少論, "Young Doctrine") followed Yun Chŭng. These were not mere debating societies. Control of a faction meant control of key ministerial posts, examination results, and the allocation of land and tax privileges. (Wikipedia: Political factions of Joseon)

Factional Violence Before Yeongjo

Under King Sukjong (r. 1674-1720), factional conflict reached murderous intensity. Sukjong pursued a strategy of rapid reversals (환국, hwanguk), elevating one faction to power and then abruptly replacing it with the other. Each reversal brought purges, exiles, and executions. The Noron-Soron slaughter of 1721-22 under King Gyeongjong left the court fractured beyond easy repair. When Prince Yeoning ascended as King Yeongjo in 1724, he inherited a government in which political affiliation was literally a matter of life and death. (Dark Side of Seoul)

Sado's tragedy was entangled with this factional system. The Noron faction, which held dominant influence at court during Yeongjo's reign, had reason to fear a crown prince who might shift the balance of power upon succession. The accusations against Sado were channeled through courtiers with factional loyalties. Whether his behavior was genuinely dangerous or politically convenient (or both) remains one of the central questions of Korean historiography, and the film leaves this tension deliberately unresolved.

Lady Hyegyeong's Memoirs: The Hanjungnok

The primary historical source for the Sado tragedy is the Hanjungnok (한중록, "Records Written in Silence"), a set of four autobiographical memoirs composed by Lady Hyegyeong (혜경궁 홍씨), Sado's wife, between 1795 and 1805. This is an extraordinary document: a first-person account by a woman of the inner court, written in Korean hangul rather than the literary Chinese used by male scholars. JaHyun Kim Haboush's annotated English translation, published by the University of California Press, received the Korean Literature Translation Award and remains the standard scholarly edition. (UC Press)

Four Memoirs, Four Purposes

  • Memoir of 1795: Addressed to her nephew. Defends her natal family (the Pungsan Hong) and her own conduct before and after Sado's death.
  • Memoir of 1801: Written in protest after the execution of her brother on false charges of Catholic conversion. Takes the form of a memorial petition.
  • Memoir of 1802: A narrative of the life and reign of her son, King Jeongjo, addressed to her grandson King Sunjo.
  • Memoir of 1805: The most detailed account of Sado's decline and death, written when Lady Hyegyeong was in her seventies.

Each memoir served a distinct political function. Lady Hyegyeong was not simply recording memories. She was intervening in ongoing factional disputes from a position of constrained but real political influence. (Journal of Asian Studies review)

The film draws heavily on the Hanjungnok, using Lady Hyegyeong (played by Moon Geun-young) as a witness figure caught between loyalty to her husband and survival for her son. The memoirs' emotional texture, combining grief, political calculation, and self-defense, gives the film its structural backbone.

Director Profile: Lee Joon-ik

Lee Joon-ik (born September 21, 1959) is one of South Korea's most accomplished directors of historical cinema. He entered the film industry not as a director but as a poster designer and marketer, eventually founding the Cineworld production company in the early 1990s. His directorial debut, Kid Cop (1993), was a family comedy. His breakthrough came a decade later with Once Upon a Time in a Battlefield (2003), which demonstrated his signature ability to mix humor with historical gravity. (PeoplePill / IMDb)

Key Filmography

  • King and the Clown (왕의 남자, 2005). A tale of two street performers entangled with the court of the tyrant Yeonsangun. Attracted 12.3 million viewers, making it one of the highest-grossing Korean films at the time. Its success proved that Korean audiences would turn out in massive numbers for serious historical drama.
  • Once Upon a Time in a Battlefield (황산벌, 2003). A comedic retelling of the 660 CE battle between Baekje and Silla, blending slapstick with genuine historical detail.
  • The Throne (사도, 2015). The Sado tragedy. 6.2 million admissions. South Korea's entry for the 88th Academy Awards.
  • Dongju: The Portrait of a Poet (동주, 2016). A black-and-white film about the poet Yun Dong-ju, who wrote in Korean under Japanese colonial rule and died in a Japanese prison in 1945.
  • The Book of Fish (자산어보, 2021). Set during the reign of King Sunjo, follows the exiled scholar Jeong Yak-jeon and a young fisherman on Heuksan Island.

(NBC News / IMDb)

Lee's body of work returns consistently to Korean history, but his approach is neither hagiographic nor cynical. He treats historical figures as complex human beings operating within systems of power that constrain their choices. In The Throne, this means refusing to cast Yeongjo as a simple villain or Sado as a simple victim. Both are trapped by the Confucian order they serve and by the factional machinery that surrounds them.

Film Analysis: Performances and Structure

Song Kang-ho as King Yeongjo

Song Kang-ho (born January 17, 1967) is widely regarded as the most important actor in Korean cinema. His collaborations with Bong Joon-ho (Memories of Murder, The Host, Snowpiercer, Parasite) established him as an actor of extraordinary range. In 2020, The New York Times named him one of the greatest actors of the 21st century. In 2022, he became the first Korean actor to win Best Actor at the Cannes Film Festival for his performance in Broker. (Google Arts & Culture)

As Yeongjo, Song delivers a performance of controlled devastation. His raspy voice and commanding presence convey a king who believes absolutely in the system he upholds, even as that system demands the death of his own son. Variety's Justin Chang described him as cutting "an anguished figure, boldly spurning the audience's sympathy while somehow earning it anyway." Song plays Yeongjo not as a monster but as a man crushed by the weight of duty, a Confucian ruler who has internalized the principle that the state supersedes the family, even when the cost is unbearable. (Variety)

Yoo Ah-in as Crown Prince Sado

Yoo Ah-in (born Uhm Hong-sik, October 6, 1986) was 28 years old when he took on the role of Sado, and it proved to be a career-defining performance. The year 2015 was dubbed the "Ah-in Era" (아인시대) by Korean entertainment media, as Yoo also starred in the blockbuster Veteran and the television series Six Flying Dragons in the same year. (IMDb)

Yoo's portrayal of Sado traces a devastating arc. He is introduced as a soft-hearted, eager-to-please young man desperate for his father's approval. Over the course of the film, he transforms into a figure of complete wretchedness and despair. Reviewers noted his ability to shift seamlessly from a heartbroken young adult to a man in the grip of violent breakdown, and finally to a person clawing at the inside of his wooden prison. The physical commitment of the performance, particularly in the rice chest sequences, was widely praised as one of the most powerful pieces of screen acting in recent Korean cinema. (SikSeekLand)

Critical Reception and Box Office

The Throne opened in South Korea on September 16, 2015, across 1,210 screens distributed by Showbox. In its first five days, it recorded 1.81 million admissions and grossed $12.3 million. By the end of its theatrical run, the film accumulated 6,247,638 admissions and grossed approximately $33.2 million domestically, securing fifth place among the highest-grossing South Korean films of 2015. (Korean Film Council (KOFIC))

The film earned strong critical notices both domestically and internationally. It won Best Film, Best Screenplay, and Best Music at the 35th Korean Association of Film Critics Awards, and received the Grand Prize (Daesang) at the 52nd Baeksang Arts Awards in 2016. It was selected as South Korea's entry for the Best Foreign Language Film at the 88th Academy Awards, though it did not advance to the final nomination. It also received a Special Jury Prize at India's International Film Festival. (KOFIC / Letterboxd)

Connection to Course Themes

The Throne engages directly with several core questions of this course. First, it dramatizes the tension between filial piety (효, hyo) and political necessity. Confucian ethics demand absolute loyalty from son to father. But what happens when the father is also the king, and the son is also a political liability? The film shows that Confucian order, taken to its logical extreme, can require a father to kill his own child in the name of the very family system that is supposed to protect him.

Second, the film exposes the machinery of factional politics. The courtiers who denounce Sado are not acting out of concern for the state. They are maneuvering for post-succession advantage. The Joseon factional system, which began as a competition among Neo-Confucian intellectual lineages, had by the 18th century become a zero-sum struggle for survival.

Confucian Order as Tragedy

Lee Joon-ik's achievement is to present the Confucian system not as an abstract philosophy but as a lived structure of power. Yeongjo does not kill his son because he is cruel. He kills his son because the system he serves leaves him no alternative that preserves both the dynasty and the succession. The rice chest is not an instrument of personal vengeance. It is a bureaucratic solution to an impossible political problem. That is what makes it so horrifying. The Throne asks its audience to consider whether a system that demands this outcome can truly be called orderly, or whether it is simply violence dressed in the language of virtue.

Third, the legacy of Prince Sado ripples forward through Korean history. His son, King Jeongjo, ascended the throne in 1776 and spent his reign attempting to rehabilitate his father's name. Jeongjo built the fortress of Hwaseong in Suwon partly to honor Sado, relocating his father's tomb nearby. The question of how a society remembers its political victims, and whether rehabilitation is possible within the same system that produced the injustice, remains relevant in Korean political culture.

Key Terms

Dwiju 뒤주
Wooden rice chest used for grain storage. The instrument of Sado's death, roughly 1.3 meters square. Chosen because it killed without defiling the royal body.
T'angp'yŏng 탕평
"Grand harmony." Yeongjo's policy of balancing rival court factions to concentrate power in the throne rather than any single political bloc.
Noron / Soron 노론 / 소론
"Old Doctrine" and "Young Doctrine." The two dominant factions of 18th-century Joseon politics, split from the Westerner party over philosophical and political disputes.
Hanjungnok 한중록
"Records Written in Silence." Lady Hyegyeong's four memoirs (1795-1805), the key primary source for the Sado incident. Written in hangul, not literary Chinese.
Hyo 효
Filial piety. The foundational Confucian virtue of devotion and obedience to one's parents. Central to the film's moral conflict.
Yŏnjwaje 연좌제
Communal punishment. Joseon legal principle by which a traitor's family could be executed or exiled. The reason Yeongjo could not formally execute Sado.

Discussion Questions

  1. Yeongjo is presented as both loving father and merciless king. How does the film argue that these two roles are irreconcilable within the Confucian political system?
  2. The rice chest is simultaneously an instrument of murder and a legal technicality designed to protect Sado's family. What does this tell us about how violence is legitimized through bureaucratic procedure?
  3. How does the film use its non-linear structure (cutting between Sado's childhood and the eight days of confinement) to complicate the question of guilt and innocence?
  4. Lady Hyegyeong's memoirs are a political document as much as a personal one. How does the film handle the problem of a primary source written by someone with a clear agenda?
  5. Compare the Noron/Soron factional system to other forms of political partisanship. What makes Joseon factionalism distinctive, and what about it feels universal?
  6. King Jeongjo spent his reign rehabilitating Sado's name. Can a political system repair injustices it has produced, or does rehabilitation always require breaking from the system that caused the harm?
