Parasite

기생충
Bong Joon-ho (봉준호) 2019 132 min Thriller / Dark Comedy Class Inequality Watch Film

Historical Context: Inequality in Modern Korea

Parasite is not simply a thriller about two families. It is a diagnosis of contemporary South Korea, a society that underwent the fastest industrialization in modern history and now confronts the social fractures that process left behind. The "Miracle on the Han River" lifted millions out of poverty between the 1960s and 1990s, but by the 2010s a new consensus had formed among Korean youth: upward mobility was a lie. They called their country Hell Joseon (헬조선), a bitter coinage comparing modern Korea to the rigid caste hierarchies of the Joseon dynasty (1392-1897). (KEIA)

The term gained traction around 2015, alongside the "spoon class theory" (수저론) that sorted citizens by birth: "gold spoon" children (금수저) inherit chaebol wealth and elite education pipelines, while "dirt spoon" children (흙수저) face precarious labor regardless of effort. Data bears this out. Total sales from just four conglomerates (Samsung, Hyundai Motor, LG, SK) account for roughly 60 percent of South Korea's GDP, while non-regular workers earn only 54.6 percent of what regular workers make. (Global Asia)

The Chaebol Problem

South Korea's family-controlled conglomerates (재벌, chaebol) drove export-led industrialization under Park Chung-hee. But the same concentration of capital that enabled rapid growth now produces what economists call a "dual economy." Large firms pay high wages and offer stable contracts. Small and medium enterprises, which employ the majority of workers, offer low wages and non-regular contracts with few protections. Over 30 percent of the Korean workforce is classified as non-regular. (Ahn, Global Asia)

Youth unemployment compounds the problem. Over 58 percent of South Korea's young people are either unemployed or underemployed. The hypercompetitive education system, which demands years of private tutoring (사교육, sagyo-yuk) costing families thousands of dollars monthly, no longer guarantees a stable career. In Parasite, the Kim family's son Ki-woo has failed the university entrance exam four times. His sister Ki-jung is equally talented but has never been able to afford formal training. Their intelligence is not the issue. Access is. (Jacobin)

The Architecture of Class

Bong Joon-ho has said he conceived Parasite as a film about spaces. The vertical axis of Seoul, a city built on hills, becomes a literal diagram of class. The wealthy Park family lives in an architect-designed modernist home high on a hillside, flooded with natural light and surrounded by a manicured garden. The impoverished Kim family lives in a banjiha (반지하), a semi-basement apartment at the bottom of a sloping alley, where their only window sits at street level and drunks urinate against the glass. (Diggit Magazine)

Vertical Movement as Narrative Engine

Every transition between these two worlds is marked by stairs. When the Kims ascend to the Park residence, the camera follows them upward into sunlight and open space. When they flee back to their own neighborhood during a rainstorm, every shot tracks downward. The cinematographer, Hong Kyung-pyo, described the strategy: "I tried to reflect the gap between the rich and the poor in the amount of sunshine. In terms of topography, if you visit the concentrated semi-basement area in the lowland and the rich area in the highland, the difference in the amount of sunlight is obvious." (IndieWire)

The Park House as Set Design

Production designer Lee Ha-jun built the Park residence as a complete set, not a location. Every element was designed to communicate control and order: clean horizontal lines, uncluttered surfaces, a palette of controlled greens and grays. The house is minimal, large, and orderly, a contrast to the semi-basement neighborhood. The garden is visible through floor-to-ceiling glass, a luxury of transparency that the Kim family (whose window exposes them to anyone walking past) can never afford. (Filmmakers Academy)

The Hidden Basement: A Third Vertical Layer

The film's most shocking reveal is a secret bunker beneath the Park house, where a man has been living for years, hidden by his wife (the Parks' former housekeeper). This adds a third layer to the vertical structure: the Parks live in the light, the Kims live at street level in shadow, and a man they do not even know exists lives in total darkness below them. The architecture of the single house contains the entire class system.

The Banjiha: From Bunker to Housing Crisis

The semi-basement apartment is not a fictional device. It is a defining feature of Seoul's housing landscape and a product of Cold War military planning. In the 1970s, the South Korean government required new residential buildings to include underground floors that could serve as bunkers in the event of a North Korean attack. As rural migration flooded Seoul with workers seeking factory and service jobs, landlords began renting these spaces illegally. In 1984, the government legalized habitation of semi-basements, creating the banjiha category. (France 24)

By the time Parasite premiered, approximately 200,000 households in Seoul lived in banjiha apartments, roughly 5 percent of the capital's residences. Nationwide, that figure reached nearly 400,000 households. These units are cheaper than above-ground apartments but carry serious health risks: chronic dampness, mold, poor ventilation, and vulnerability to flooding. (NBC News)

The 2022 Floods and the Ban

In August 2022, record rainfall hit Seoul and killed at least 11 people. Three of those deaths occurred in a single flooded banjiha apartment, a family trapped in rising water in the Gwanak district. The tragedy prompted the Seoul Metropolitan Government to announce a phased ban on new banjiha construction. Officials pledged to eliminate all semi-basement housing within 20 years and provide public housing alternatives for displaced residents. (Gizmodo / NBC News)

Director Profile: Bong Joon-ho

Born September 14, 1969, in Daegu, South Korea, Bong Joon-ho studied sociology at Yonsei University before enrolling at the Korean Academy of Film Arts. His father, Bong Sang-gyun, was a graphic designer and professor of art at Yeungnam University. The sociological training proved formative. Every one of Bong's films treats systemic structures (police bureaucracy, environmental crisis, global capitalism, class hierarchy) as the true subject, with genre serving as the delivery mechanism. (Britannica)

Key Filmography

Five Films Before Parasite

  • Memories of Murder (2003). Based on South Korea's first serial murder case (1986-1991), set during the authoritarian Fifth Republic. Became the highest-grossing Korean film of 2003 and is now considered one of the greatest crime films ever made.
  • The Host (2006). A monster film triggered by U.S. military pollution of the Han River (based on a real 2000 incident). Became the highest-grossing film in South Korean history at the time of its release.
  • Mother (2009). A thriller about a widow navigating a corrupt legal system to free her intellectually disabled son.
  • Snowpiercer (2013). Bong's English-language debut. A train carrying the last survivors of a frozen Earth is stratified by class, with the poor in the back and the rich in the front. The vertical metaphor of Parasite runs horizontally here.
  • Okja (2017). A Netflix-produced film about the global meat industry and corporate manipulation. Screened in competition at Cannes.

(Britannica / IMDB)

Across this body of work, Bong developed a signature method: genre films that operate as social critique without ever becoming didactic. His tonal control, shifting between comedy, horror, and pathos within a single scene, is often cited as what makes his work accessible to global audiences while remaining rooted in specifically Korean social conditions.

Cultural Impact and the Korean Wave

Parasite premiered at the 2019 Cannes Film Festival and won the Palme d'Or by unanimous jury vote, the first South Korean film to receive the prize. It then swept the 92nd Academy Awards in February 2020, winning four Oscars: Best Picture, Best Director, Best Original Screenplay, and Best International Feature Film. It was the first non-English-language film to win Best Picture in the Academy's 92-year history. (PBS NewsHour)

"Once you overcome the one-inch-tall barrier of subtitles, you will be introduced to so many more amazing films."

Bong Joon-ho, 2020 Golden Globe acceptance speech

The film's global success represented a new peak for hallyu (한류, the Korean Wave), the broad diffusion of South Korean cultural products across global markets. Korean Wave exports rose 22.4 percent in 2019 from the previous year, and Parasite became a symbol of Korean cinema's ability to produce work that was simultaneously local in its social specificity and universal in its themes. Produced on an $11.4 million budget, the film grossed $263 million worldwide ($71.4 million in South Korea, $53.4 million in North America, and $133.9 million in other territories). (PMC / The Numbers)

Connection to Course Themes

Parasite connects directly to several threads running through this course. First, it dramatizes the consequences of South Korea's compressed modernization. The chaebol-driven export economy that Park Chung-hee initiated in the 1960s created enormous wealth but concentrated it in a small number of family dynasties. Second, the film inverts Confucian hierarchy: the Kim family performs deference and loyalty (the classic subordinate virtues) as a con, exposing how the language of respect functions as a tool of class maintenance rather than moral order. Third, the "smell" that the Parks detect on the Kims (and that Mr. Kim eventually murders over) is the smell of the banjiha itself: dampness, mold, poverty encoded in the body. Class in Korea is not only economic. It is sensory, spatial, and biological.

From "Miracle" to "Hell Joseon"

The arc from the developmental state to Parasite can be traced in a single statistic. In 1960, South Korea's GDP per capita was roughly equivalent to Ghana's. By 2019, it was the 12th-largest economy in the world. But the Gini coefficient for income inequality has risen steadily since the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, and housing prices in Seoul have become among the most unaffordable on Earth relative to average income. The "miracle" produced its own negation: a generation that sees no future in the system their parents built. (Georgetown GJIA)

Key Terms

Banjiha 반지하
Semi-basement apartment. Originally built as Cold War bunkers in the 1970s, legalized for habitation in 1984. About 200,000 households in Seoul lived in banjiha units as of 2019.
Chaebol 재벌
Family-controlled conglomerate. Samsung, Hyundai, LG, and SK together account for roughly 60% of South Korea's GDP. The word combines "wealth" (재) and "clan" (벌).
Hell Joseon 헬조선
Satirical term (c. 2015) comparing modern Korea's rigid class system to the Joseon dynasty. Expresses youth frustration with impossible housing costs, education arms races, and precarious labor.
Sujeori 수저론
"Spoon class theory." Classifies people as gold spoon (금수저, born wealthy) or dirt spoon (흙수저, born poor), arguing that birth determines outcomes more than effort.
Hallyu 한류
The "Korean Wave." The global spread of South Korean popular culture (film, music, TV, food). Parasite's Oscar wins marked a high point for Korean cinema within this broader phenomenon.

Discussion Questions

  1. How does the vertical geography of the film (basement, ground floor, hilltop) map onto the class structure of contemporary Seoul?
  2. The Kim family is resourceful, creative, and skilled. Why does the film suggest that individual talent is insufficient to escape poverty?
  3. Mr. Park repeatedly mentions that Ki-taek (the father) "crosses the line" in terms of smell and personal boundaries. What is Bong saying about how class is perceived through the body rather than just the wallet?
  4. Compare Parasite's depiction of a stratified train/building to Snowpiercer's horizontal class system. What does the shift from horizontal to vertical suggest about Bong's evolving analysis?
  5. How does the "Hell Joseon" discourse among Korean youth challenge the developmental state narrative that economic growth lifts all boats?
  6. The Parks are not portrayed as villains. They are "nice." What argument is the film making about structural violence versus individual morality?
