Deep Dive: Documentary

Loyal Citizens of Pyongyang in Seoul

평양의 충성스러운 시민들, 서울에서
David Yun, 2018 38 minutes Documentary, Short United States / South Korea Watch Film

Historical Context: North Korean Defectors in South Korea

The Defector Pipeline

Since the end of the Korean War in 1953, tens of thousands of North Koreans have made their way to South Korea. The cumulative total reached 34,538 by the end of 2025, with annual arrivals peaking at 2,914 in 2009 before declining sharply after the COVID-19 pandemic closed borders. Only 224 arrived in 2025.[1]

The journey itself is harrowing. Most defectors cannot cross the heavily fortified DMZ directly. Instead, they travel through China (often via brokers who charge thousands of dollars), then onward through Southeast Asian countries before reaching a South Korean embassy or consulate. Women make up roughly 70-80% of all defectors, many of whom are vulnerable to trafficking during transit through China.[2]

What happens after arrival is less well known, and it is this process that the documentary interrogates. Upon reaching South Korea, defectors enter a multi-stage system controlled first by the National Intelligence Service (NIS), then by the Ministry of Unification. The film argues that this system is not merely administrative. It is a mechanism for producing narratives.

The Resettlement Process

Every North Korean who arrives in South Korea passes through three stages. First, interrogation by the NIS at the Joint Interrogation Center (합동신문센터), lasting up to 90 days. The stated purpose is to screen for spies. During this period, defectors are held in isolation and questioned extensively about their backgrounds, motives, and knowledge of the DPRK.[3]

Second, a mandatory 12-week course at Hanawon (하나원), the Settlement Support Center for North Korean Refugees. Opened in July 1999 in Anseong, Gyeonggi Province, Hanawon provides language training, vocational education, and instruction in South Korean economics and political systems. Critics, including the film's subjects, describe this stage as ideological re-education rather than neutral resettlement support.[4]

Third, release into South Korean society with a settlement payment of approximately 7-8 million won (roughly $5,000-6,000) and access to housing assistance. From this point, defectors must navigate a society where they face unemployment rates more than double the national average (6.3% vs. 3% in 2024), widespread discrimination, and a suicide rate roughly twice that of South Korean citizens.[5]

The Documentary's Argument

Questioning the Testimony Industry

The central claim of Loyal Citizens of Pyongyang in Seoul is that North Korean defector testimony, as it circulates in Western and South Korean media, is not simply raw truth from people who escaped oppression. It is a product, shaped by institutional pressures, financial incentives, and geopolitical interests. The film does not argue that all defector accounts are fabricated. It argues that the system through which those accounts are produced and disseminated systematically distorts them.

The film opens with South Korean human rights lawyer Jang Kyong-ook, who describes how the National Security Act has been used to coerce confessions and manufacture "spy" cases from defectors. According to Jang, North Korean individuals held in NIS detention centers are subjected to solitary confinement for months, after which they become desperate enough to sign or say whatever is required to secure their release.[6]

Yun then meets two defectors living in Seoul, identified as Mr. Choi and Mrs. Kim. Their accounts complicate the familiar narrative of grateful escapees building new lives in freedom. They describe coercion, disillusionment, and in some cases, a desire to return to the North. The film presents their testimony without editorial framing, allowing the contradictions between their lived experience and the dominant media narrative to speak for themselves.[7]

The NIS and the Production of Defectors

A significant portion of the documentary examines the role of the National Intelligence Service in managing defectors. The NIS (formerly the KCIA, then the National Security Planning Agency) has overseen North Korean defector affairs for over 30 years. This is an intelligence agency with a well-documented history of political repression, including the kidnapping and torture of dissidents during the authoritarian era.[3]

The film's most provocative claim is that the NIS does not merely process defectors but actively shapes what they say publicly. The film points to the 2016 case of 13 North Korean restaurant workers in Ningbo, China, who were brought to South Korea in what the South Korean government called a group defection. The restaurant's manager, Heo Gang-il, later told JTBC and CNN that he had tricked the women into leaving at the NIS's direction. "It was luring and kidnapping, and I know because I took the lead," he said. South Korea's National Human Rights Commission subsequently found that the women's basic rights had been violated.[8]

The documentary connects these institutional mechanisms to a broader pattern: defectors whose testimony aligns with anti-DPRK narratives receive media platforms, speaking invitations, and financial support, while those whose accounts are more ambiguous or who express any positive memories of life in the North are marginalized.

Director Profile

David Yun

David Yun made Loyal Citizens of Pyongyang in Seoul as a UCLA undergraduate student. The film, completed in 2018, is a 38-minute short documentary shot primarily in Seoul with additional research conducted in the United States. His other credits include Hold the Sun (2009) and As a Gentleman Would Say (2006).[9]

The film's production values are modest. It relies almost entirely on talking-head interviews, archival footage, and on-screen text. Reviewers on Letterboxd have described it as "amateurish" in production quality but "passionate" in substance. One reviewer gave it three stars for craft but five for educational value.[10]

Yun's approach is openly polemical. The film concludes by tracing connections between prominent North Korean defectors and right-wing think tanks in Washington, arguing that defector testimony has been instrumentalized to serve U.S. foreign policy objectives. This is not a film that pretends to neutrality, and its value for classroom discussion lies partly in that transparency. Students can engage with its claims on the merits rather than mistaking advocacy for objectivity.[6]

Testimony and Its Uses

The Reliability Problem

The question of defector testimony reliability predates and extends well beyond this film. A 2015 Washington Post investigation found that cash payments for defector interviews are standard practice, with more dramatic or emotional accounts commanding higher fees. This creates a market incentive to embellish. Some defectors have acknowledged tailoring their stories to meet audience expectations.[11]

The most prominent case is that of Shin Dong-hyuk, whose memoir Escape from Camp 14 became a bestseller and whose testimony was cited in the 2014 UN Commission of Inquiry report on DPRK human rights. In January 2015, Shin admitted to changing key details of his account, including which prison camp he was held in and the timeline of his escape attempts. His author, Blaine Harden, subsequently described Shin as an "unreliable narrator." The UN Commission's chairman noted, however, that Shin's testimony comprised only two paragraphs of a 400-page report based on hundreds of witnesses.[12]

The reliability problem is genuine but easily oversimplified. Trauma itself distorts memory. Many defectors have experienced severe psychological stress comparable to combat PTSD, and the journey out through China involves additional layers of trauma. The question is not whether defector testimony is "true or false" but how institutional systems shape which stories are told, amplified, and believed.

Propaganda on Both Sides

The film's framing invites a broader observation about how the Korean division generates propaganda in both directions. The DPRK uses returning defectors (or those it claims chose to return) for its own television broadcasts, presenting them as people who saw the corruption of the South and chose the embrace of socialism. South Korea and its allies, conversely, platform defectors whose accounts emphasize the horrors of the North, reinforcing narratives that justify military deterrence and economic sanctions.[2]

Neither version captures the full picture. The DPRK is a state that has committed documented human rights abuses, including political prison camps and severe restrictions on movement and expression. But the South Korean and American use of defector testimony is not a neutral process of "letting survivors speak." It is a curated system in which some stories are amplified and others suppressed, based on their alignment with geopolitical objectives. The film's contribution is to make this curation visible.[6]

Critical Reception

A Polarizing Film

The documentary has generated sharply divided responses. It holds a 7.5 rating on IMDb.[13] On Letterboxd, reviews are polarized along political lines. Viewers sympathetic to the film's left-critical perspective praise it for challenging what they see as Western propaganda about North Korea. Those who find the film's framing too sympathetic to the DPRK argue that it minimizes genuine suffering.[10]

The Artifice published a detailed analysis titled "Understanding Enemies of the Empire," which engaged seriously with the film's claims about NIS coercion and the testimony industry while noting its ideological commitments.[6] The blog Under Southern Eyes described the film as exposing "how South Korea and the US use North Korean defectors as propaganda tools."[7]

For course purposes, the film's partisanship is a feature rather than a bug. It forces students to grapple with a perspective that most Western media excludes entirely. The productive question is not "is this film right or wrong?" but "what does this film see that other accounts do not, and what does it miss?"

Classroom Value: Who Speaks for North Koreans?

The documentary connects to one of the deepest questions in Korean division studies: who has the authority to speak about North Korean society? Defectors have direct experience but face institutional pressures that shape their public testimony. Western journalists and scholars have analytical frameworks but lack direct access. The DPRK government controls information absolutely but has its own obvious agenda. South Korean intelligence agencies possess enormous data but operate in secrecy and with geopolitical motivations.

No single source is reliable on its own. The film's value lies in making students skeptical of all single-source narratives, including the film itself. Paired with more conventional accounts of defector experiences, it produces a richer, more honest conversation about the human cost of division.

Key Terms

T'albukja / T'albungmin (탈북자 / 탈북민)
Literally "person who fled the North." The most common informal term for North Korean defectors in South Korea. In 2005, the Ministry of Unification briefly introduced saeteomin (새터민, "people of new land") as a replacement. In March 2026, the ministry adopted "North Korean-born citizens" as the official English rendering. The shifting terminology reflects ongoing debates about identity, agency, and political framing. [Wikipedia]
Hanawon (하나원)
The Settlement Support Center for North Korean Refugees, located in Anseong, Gyeonggi Province. Opened July 8, 1999. All defectors must complete a mandatory 12-week program covering language, vocational training, and "social adaptation." Critics describe it as ideological re-education. As defector numbers have declined, the government plans to merge its Hwacheon branch into the Anseong facility. [Crossing Borders]
NIS (국가정보원, National Intelligence Service)
South Korea's primary intelligence agency, formerly the KCIA (1961-1981) and NSPA (1981-1999). Responsible for the initial interrogation of all North Korean defectors, lasting up to 90 days. Has a documented history of political repression, including fabricating spy cases and coercing confessions under the National Security Act. The film alleges NIS involvement in manipulating defector testimony. [Wikipedia]
Songbun (성분)
North Korea's hereditary social classification system, originating from a 1957 Workers' Party Politburo resolution. Divides all citizens into three castes: core (약 25%), wavering (약 55%), and hostile (약 20%), based on the perceived political loyalty of one's family going back three generations. Determines access to food, education, housing, and employment. During the 1994-1998 famine, songbun often determined who ate and who starved. [HRNK]
National Security Act (국가보안법)
South Korean law enacted in 1948, originally targeting communist sympathizers. Remains in force. Criminalizes praising or sympathizing with the DPRK. Human rights lawyer Jang Kyong-ook argues in the film that the act has been used to coerce false confessions from defectors and to manufacture "spy" cases that reinforce narratives of North Korean infiltration.

Discussion Questions

1. The film argues that defector testimony is shaped by institutional pressures (NIS interrogation, financial incentives, media markets). Does acknowledging these pressures necessarily invalidate the testimony itself? Where is the line between healthy skepticism and dismissal?
2. Director David Yun is openly polemical rather than neutral. How does his transparency about his perspective compare to media that presents itself as objective while also making editorial choices about which defector stories to amplify?
3. The 2016 Ningbo restaurant case involved 13 North Korean women whose "defection" was later described by their own manager as NIS-orchestrated kidnapping. What does this case reveal about the gap between how defection is presented publicly and how it may actually occur?
4. Both North and South Korea use defector testimony for propaganda purposes. How does the instrumentalization of personal suffering differ between the two states? Is there a meaningful moral distinction, or is it the same mechanism pointed in different directions?
5. North Korean defectors in South Korea face unemployment rates more than double the national average and a suicide rate roughly twice that of South Korean citizens. What do these statistics suggest about the gap between the narrative of "freedom" and the lived reality of resettlement?
6. Who has the authority to speak about life in North Korea? Defectors face institutional pressure. Scholars lack direct access. The DPRK government controls information. Western intelligence agencies have agendas. How should we navigate this epistemological problem?
