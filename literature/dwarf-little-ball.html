Deep Dive: Novel / Story Cycle — Week 6

A Dwarf Launches a Little Ball

난장이가 쏘아올린 작은 공  ·  Cho Sehui
Cho Sehui (조세희, 1942–2022) 1978 (stories published 1975–78) Novel / linked story cycle South Korea

About the Author

The Writer of One Singular Achievement

Cho Sehui (조세희) was born in 1942 and spent years writing and publishing the twelve interconnected stories that make up Nanjangi (난장이가 쏘아올린 작은 공, "A Dwarf Launches a Little Ball"), individually in literary journals between 1975 and 1978. The collected volume appeared in 1978 and became one of the best-selling and most influential books in South Korean history, selling millions of copies and becoming essential reading for the labor and pro-democracy movements of the 1980s. Cho published very little afterward. Nanjangi is his singular contribution, and it is enough.

About the Text

The Dwarf's Family and Urban Redevelopment

The book follows several families, most centrally the family of a man called "the dwarf" (난장이, nanjangi) — a small, disabled man who works various menial jobs and lives with his wife and children in a working-class neighborhood in Seoul. The neighborhood has been designated for urban redevelopment: the government will tear it down to build the apartment complexes that are the sign of Korea's modernization. The dwarf's family will receive a housing certificate — a piece of paper that theoretically entitles them to a new apartment, but which they have no practical means to convert into actual housing. They will be displaced.

This scenario — working-class families expelled from the city's center to make room for middle-class apartment complexes — was not fictional. It was the actual experience of hundreds of thousands of Seoul residents in the 1970s, as the Park Chung-hee government and private developers demolished old neighborhoods in a coordinated program of urban modernization. Cho Sehui made it visible and gave it faces.

The Little Ball: Aspiration and Gravity

The title image — a dwarf launching a little ball skyward — is one of the most resonant in modern Korean literature. The dwarf is small, powerless, crushed by the weight of the social order. The ball is his aspiration, his hope, the small and fragile dream of a better life. He launches it skyward. And it falls back down, because this is the physical world, and in the physical world, what goes up comes down. Aspiration without structural change is just a temporary suspension of gravity.

The image holds together beauty, tragedy, and structural argument simultaneously. It does not say: the dwarf should not have hopes. It says: the system he lives in is designed to ensure that his hopes cannot be fulfilled. The tragedy is not individual failure but systemic design.

Formal Experimentation: Fractured Narration

Cho Sehui's narrative method in Nanjangi is deliberately non-linear and fragmented. The twelve stories are not organized chronologically or around a single narrator. Perspectives shift; the same events are seen from different angles; voices interrupt and contradict each other. This formal choice reflects the lives of the characters — people whose experience is fragmented by displacement, poverty, and the chaos of rapid urbanization, who do not have access to the coherent, continuous narrative arc that middle-class life might provide.

It is also a formal refusal: the novel will not provide the satisfying narrative closure that would allow readers to feel the problems it depicts have been resolved. They have not been resolved. The form insists on this.

Literature as Labor Movement Text

Nanjangi became a text of the Korean labor movement in the 1980s — read by factory workers, discussed in study circles, cited in speeches and manifestos. This appropriation raises a question the course returns to repeatedly: what does it mean for a literary text to become politically functional? Does the text change when it is read as organizing material rather than as art? Does the act of canonization as "movement text" freeze a work's meaning in ways that limit interpretation?

Cho Sehui did not write a manifesto. He wrote twelve stories about specific people in a specific place and time. The political reading is a reading — a true and important one — but not the only reading available.

Key Concepts

Sanggye-dong and urban redevelopment
Seoul's urban redevelopment in the 1970s involved forced removal of working-class residents from desirable inner-city land to make way for apartment complexes. Residents received housing certificates they often could not use. Cho Sehui's book was inspired by these real events and the communities destroyed by them.
Nanjangi (난장이, dwarf)
The dwarf as protagonist is not simply a sympathetic figure — he is a structural metaphor. In a society organized around size (economic power, political power, social status), the dwarf is the most compressed symbol of those who are systematically made small. But he also has dignity, dreams, and a family: the novel insists on his full humanity.
Jeonse (전세) and housing rights
The Korean housing system involves complex lease arrangements, including jeonse (전세, a large deposit payment in lieu of rent). The dwarf family's housing certificate — a government-issued document meant to compensate for urban displacement — is a piece of bureaucratic paper disconnected from any practical possibility of securing housing. The gap between the paper and the reality is the story's structural argument.

Discussion Questions

1. Why a dwarf? What does physical smallness signify in this novel — and who in the story are the giants?
2. The "little ball" is launched skyward and must fall back down. Is this image hopeful, tragic, or both?
3. Cho Sehui uses fragmented, non-linear narration. How does this formal choice reflect the lives of his characters?
4. Compare this novel with Kim Jiha's "Five Bandits." They address 1970s Korea in completely different registers. What does each form allow that the other cannot?
5. This book became a text of the labor movement. How does a literary text become politically functional — and what risks does that carry for the text itself?
