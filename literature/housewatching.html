Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 5

The Day My Housewatching Ended

집보기는 그 어렵더니  ·  Pak Wanso
Pak Wanso (박완서, 1931–2011) 1978 Short Story South Korea

About the Author

Pak Wanso's Trilogy of Domestic Critique

This story belongs to the same period and project as "Identical Apartments" (1974), also assigned in Week 5. Together, the two stories form part of Pak Wanso's sustained literary examination of what South Korea's rapid development and urbanization did to domestic space and to the women who inhabited it. The third story in this cluster — also in Week 4 — is "A Pasque-Flower on That Bleak Day" (1977), which brings the Korean War's long shadow into the same domestic frame.

Reading these three stories together reveals the range of Pak Wanso's project: war memory, urban alienation, and class-stratified domestic labor are not separate subjects but interlocking dimensions of the same social reality she is trying to represent.

About the Text

Housewatching: Invisible Labor Made Visible

"Housewatching" (집보기, jipbogi) is the practice of watching over someone else's house — a domestic labor arrangement in which a woman (usually) stays in a house she does not own, managing it in the owner's absence in exchange for housing or payment. It is a form of domestic service that exists at the intersection of class, gender, and housing precarity: the housewatcher is simultaneously a tenant, a servant, and a kind of domestic worker, but without the legal or social recognition that any of those categories would provide.

The story uses this specific, under-recognized form of labor as a window into the broader question of what domestic work is, how it is valued (and systematically undervalued), and what the class relationships between women look like when one woman's domestic stability depends on serving another woman's household. The story's ending — "the day it ended" — signals that this particular arrangement has come to a close. What exactly ends, and what meaning that ending carries, is the story's central question.

Class Relations Between Women

One of the recurring tensions in feminist analysis is the question of solidarity and its limits: can women across class lines recognize their common interest, or do class relations between women reproduce the same structures of exploitation as class relations between men and women? Pak Wanso approaches this question through narrative rather than argument, showing the specific texture of the relationship between a housewatcher and the woman whose house she watches — the daily negotiations, the asymmetries of power, the moments of apparent solidarity and their failure.

The story does not offer a programmatic answer. It observes, with Pak Wanso's characteristic precision, what actually happens in this relationship — and that observation is more useful than any theoretical position, because it makes the reader do the analytical work.

Development Korea and Domestic Service

The rapid urbanization of South Korea in the 1960s–70s created a large market for domestic service: rural women migrating to Seoul who worked as live-in housekeepers, cooks, and caretakers (식모, sikmo) for urban middle-class families. This was one of the major forms of female labor in the development period, largely invisible in the official statistics and political discourse of the "economic miracle."

Pak Wanso's housewatching story engages this broader context without romanticizing or simplifying it. The specific arrangement she depicts is not quite the same as the sikmo relationship, but it belongs to the same world of informal, unrecognized, class-asymmetric domestic labor that characterized women's economic lives in development-era Korea.

Key Concepts

Jipbogi (집보기, housewatching)
The practice of residing in and managing another person's home in their absence, in exchange for housing or compensation. An informal domestic labor arrangement common in Korean urban life, existing outside formal employment categories.
Sikmo (식모, domestic servant)
Live-in domestic workers in Korean households — typically young women from rural areas who cooked, cleaned, and cared for children in exchange for room, board, and small wages. A major form of female labor in development-era South Korea, largely absent from official economic accounts.
Reproductive labor
The category of feminist political economy that encompasses domestic work, caregiving, and social reproduction — the labor that maintains and reproduces the workforce, which is structurally necessary to capitalism but largely uncompensated and socially invisible. Pak Wanso's domestic fiction consistently makes this labor visible.

Discussion Questions

1. What work does "housewatching" stand in for in this story? What kinds of labor are made visible and invisible?
2. How does the story represent class relations between women?
3. Compare this story with "Identical Apartments." How does Pak Wanso use domestic space differently across the two stories?
4. The story's title signals an ending — "the day it ended." What ends, and what does that ending mean?
5. How does Pak Wanso's focus on domestic life connect to larger questions about Korea's authoritarian development period?
