Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 2

Awakening

자각  ·  Kim Wonju (Il-yŏp)
Kim Wonju (김원주, 1896–1971) 1926 Short Story Colonial Korea

About the Author

Feminist, Editor, Buddhist Nun

Kim Wonju (김원주) was born in 1896, the same year as Na Hyesŏk, and together with Na she formed the core of colonial Korea's early feminist literary world. Writing under the pen name Il-yŏp (일엽, "one leaf"), she was a prolific journalist and literary figure in the 1910s and 20s. In 1920 she founded Sinyŏja (신여자, "New Woman"), Korea's first explicitly feminist journal, which published for only one year before folding but had enormous impact on Korean women's intellectual life.

After a tumultuous personal life — divorce, the death of a son — Kim Wonju took Buddhist vows in 1933 under the Sŏn master Man'gong (만공) at Sudŏk-sa monastery. She became a respected Buddhist practitioner and writer, publishing theological reflections on Buddhism and female renunciation late in life. She died in 1971, having outlived the feminist world she helped create and the colonial system that shaped it. Her life is one of the most striking trajectories in modern Korean cultural history: feminist activist to Buddhist nun.

Sinyŏja and the Feminist Press

Sinyŏja ("New Woman"), the journal Kim Wonju founded in 1920, was a landmark in Korean print culture. Publishing women's essays, fiction, and commentary on social issues — including divorce, education, and sexuality — it carved out a public space for women's intellectual expression at a moment when that space was vigorously contested. The journal ran for only four issues before financial and political difficulties ended it, but it established Kim Wonju as a major voice in colonial Korean feminism and shaped the generation of women writers who followed.

About the Text

What Does "Awakening" Mean?

"Awakening" (자각, jagak) is a richly polysemous word in the Korean context. In the feminist discourse of colonial Korea, it meant coming to consciousness about women's social oppression and one's own capacity for self-determination — the kind of awakening Kyŏnghŭi undergoes in Na Hyesŏk's story. But jagak also carries Buddhist resonance: awakening as liberation from illusion, as the recognition of the nature of mind and suffering. Kim Wonju's story operates in the space between these two meanings.

Written in 1926, after the New Woman movement's early burst of energy but before Kim Wonju's own turn toward Buddhism, the story captures a figure in transition: a protagonist who has woken to her own oppression but has not yet found a path through it. The gap between recognition and liberation is the story's subject.

Colonialism as Unmarked Pressure

Like much literature of the colonial period, "Awakening" does not directly address Japanese rule — Japanese censorship made explicit anti-colonial writing dangerous, and the story's concerns are ostensibly personal and social rather than nationalist. But colonialism shapes the story's context in ways that cannot be extracted from it. The educational institutions that created the New Woman were colonial institutions. The public sphere in which women were claiming space was a colonial public sphere. The very concept of "individual rights" being mobilized in the feminist argument was arriving through Japan from Euro-American liberal thought, filtered through the specific distortions of colonial mediation.

Reading the story against this background enriches rather than undermines its feminist argument. Kim Wonju was not naive about the complexities of her situation.

The Buddhist Turn

From Feminist Activism to Monastic Renunciation

Kim Wonju's turn to Buddhism in 1933 has been read in contradictory ways. Some feminist scholars see it as a retreat — a withdrawal from the political struggle for women's rights into a form of spiritual privatism. Others see it as a different kind of radicalism: a wholesale rejection of the social world that had punished her for claiming freedom, in favor of a framework that transcended the terms of that world entirely.

Kim Wonju's own writings about her Buddhist practice suggest the second reading is closer to her intentions. She wrote extensively about Buddhist teachings on the liberation of consciousness from suffering — and she explicitly connected this to her earlier feminist work, arguing that both were projects of liberation from false constraint. The continuity between the feminist and the nun is more legible than the rupture.

Reading "Awakening" in light of this trajectory is illuminating, but the interpretive arrow should point in both directions: the feminist story throws light on the Buddhist life, and the Buddhist life throws light on what "awakening" was always pointing toward in the story.

Key Concepts

Sinyŏja (신여자, "New Woman" journal)
Korea's first feminist journal, founded by Kim Wonju in 1920. Published for four issues, it established a Korean feminist print culture and gave women writers a venue independent of male-edited publications.
Jagak (자각, awakening/self-awareness)
The Korean word for "awakening" carries both secular (feminist consciousness) and Buddhist (liberation from illusion) resonances — a polysemy that structures Kim Wonju's literary and spiritual career.
Sŏn Buddhism (선불교)
The Korean form of Chan/Zen Buddhism, emphasizing direct mind-to-mind transmission and meditative practice. Kim Wonju studied under the great Sŏn master Man'gong, one of the revival figures of Korean Buddhism in the early twentieth century.

Discussion Questions

1. What does "awakening" mean in this story? What does the protagonist wake up to — and what does she still not see?
2. Kim Wonju later became a Buddhist nun. Does anything in this story foreshadow that path?
3. Compare this story with Na Hyesŏk's "Kyŏnghŭi." How do the two authors construct female autonomy differently?
4. How does the story position education as a tool of liberation or a source of new constraints?
5. The text was written under Japanese colonial rule. How does colonialism shape the story's context even when not explicitly named?
