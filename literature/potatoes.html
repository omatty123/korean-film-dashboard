Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 2

Potatoes

감자 (Kamja)  ·  Kim Tongin
Kim Tongin (김동인, 1900–1951) 1925 Short Story Colonial Korea

About the Author

Pioneer of Korean Literary Naturalism

Kim Tongin (김동인) was born in 1900 into a wealthy merchant family in Pyongyang and educated in Japan, where he encountered the French and Japanese naturalist literary traditions — Émile Zola, Guy de Maupassant, and their Japanese adapters. In 1919 he co-founded the literary journal Ch'angjo (창조, "Creation"), widely considered the first modern Korean literary journal, which promoted Western literary naturalism and realism as models for a new Korean prose fiction.

Kim Tongin's fiction is concerned with the deterministic relationship between environment, heredity, and human fate. His characters are often destroyed not by their own choices but by the conditions into which they were born — a literary philosophy that was radical in its implications: if environment determines fate, then poverty and colonial exploitation are causes of human degradation, not symptoms of personal failing.

About the Text

The Slums of Colonial Pyongyang

Kamja (감자, Potatoes) is set in the slum districts on the outskirts of Pyongyang — a city that, under Japanese colonial rule, was developing rapidly as an industrial center in the northwest of the peninsula. The slums housed those left behind by colonial modernization: displaced peasants, families broken by poverty, people existing at the absolute margin of the colonial economy.

The story's protagonist, Bok-nyeo (복녀), comes from a family that had some respectability but was destroyed by poverty and her feckless husband's failures. Her descent into the slums triggers a chain of consequences that the story presents as essentially inevitable — determined by her environment. She begins selling sexual access to survive; the logic of survival in the slum becomes the logic of her life; the story ends in her violent death.

Naturalist Determinism: A Literary Framework

Kim Tongin's naturalism involves a specific claim about human agency: that environment and circumstance are more powerful than individual will. This is not nihilism but a structural argument. In Zola's framework (and Kim Tongin's adaptation of it), showing how poverty destroys a person is an indictment of the conditions that produce poverty — not a justification of those conditions. The cold, clinical narrative distance Kim Tongin maintains is a formal choice: the narrator will not sentimentalize what is happening, because sentimentality would make it personal rather than structural.

This framework has limitations that the story itself sometimes exposes. Naturalist determinism can shade into a dehumanizing gaze — one that observes suffering from a distance without asking what the observed person thinks, feels, or wants. Bok-nyeo's interiority remains largely opaque. The story tells us what happens to her; it is less interested in what she makes of it.

Colonialism, Poverty, and Gender

"Potatoes" is a story about a woman, but it is not a feminist story in the mode of Na Hyesŏk or Kim Wonju. Kim Tongin observes Bok-nyeo from outside; his concern is with her as a product of her environment, not with her inner life or her claims to self-determination. This contrast with the New Woman stories is instructive. Where Na Hyesŏk and Kim Wonju write about educated elite women claiming rights, Kim Tongin writes about a poor woman for whom such claims are not available — because poverty has foreclosed the educational and social prerequisites for making them.

The two sets of texts together raise a question the course pursues throughout: whose liberation was at stake in colonial Korea's various emancipatory movements? Which women were imagined as the subjects of feminist writing, and which were more often objects of it?

Key Concepts

Literary naturalism (자연주의)
A literary movement associated with Zola and the French 19th-century novel, emphasizing the determining influence of heredity and environment on human behavior. Kim Tongin adapted this framework for colonial Korean fiction, focusing on poverty and class as the forces that destroy his characters.
Ch'angjo (창조, "Creation")
Korea's first modern literary journal, founded in 1919 by Kim Tongin, Chu Yohan, and others. Published only nine issues but established the infrastructure for modern Korean literature — introducing Western literary forms and norms of craft to Korean readers and writers.
Pyongyang under colonialism
Pyongyang (평양) was a major industrial and commercial center under Japanese colonial rule, heavily developed after 1910. Its rapid urbanization generated the kind of urban poverty Kim Tongin depicts — displaced rural migrants crowded into slum districts at the margins of the colonial city.

Discussion Questions

1. Kim Tongin presents Bok-nyeo's fate as almost inevitable — determined by her environment. Do you find this convincing, or does determinism risk becoming an excuse for structural injustice?
2. How does the story represent the relationship between colonialism and poverty? Is the colonial system an explicit presence or an invisible one?
3. Compare Bok-nyeo's situation with the women in Na Hyesŏk's and Kim Wonju's stories. What different visions of women's possibilities appear across these texts?
4. The story is narrated from a clinical, almost detached distance. What is the effect of this narrative choice — and what does it obscure?
5. How does setting — the Pyongyang slums — function as a character in this story?
