Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 7

Salt

소금 (Sogŭm)  ·  Kang Gyeongae
Kang Gyeongae (강경애, 1906–1944) 1934 Short Story Colonial Korea / Manchuria

About the Author

The Proletarian Writer of the Manchurian Diaspora

Kang Gyeongae (강경애) was born in 1906 in Hwanghae Province in what is now North Korea. She received limited formal education — a biographical fact she turned into literary subject matter, writing about women excluded from the educational opportunities that shaped writers like Na Hyesŏk. She spent much of her adult life in Manchuria (Gando/Jiandao, now China's Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture), where hundreds of thousands of Koreans had migrated to escape colonial poverty and land displacement.

She died in 1944 at 37, of illness, before liberation. Her death before the end of colonial rule gave her writing a specific historical position: she wrote from inside colonialism, without the perspective of having survived it. Her major works — including the novel Human Problem (인간문제, 1934) and the story The Underground Village (지하촌, 1936) — are among the most powerful socialist realist works of the colonial period. She died before she could be classified as North or South Korean, which may partly explain why she remained relatively unknown for decades compared to her male contemporaries.

Manchuria and the Korean Diaspora

The Korean diaspora in Manchuria (Gando, 간도 in Korean; Jiandao in Chinese) was one of the defining facts of colonial Korean life. Hundreds of thousands of Koreans migrated to Manchuria during the 1910s–30s, pushed out by the colonial land reform that dispossessed Korean peasants and pulled by the possibility of farming relatively unclaimed land in Manchuria. By the 1930s, there were hundreds of thousands of Koreans in the Yanbian region, maintaining Korean language and culture while existing in a complex position relative to both the Japanese colonial state and the Chinese population.

Life for these migrants was extremely harsh. They farmed land in brutal winters, faced discrimination from both Japanese authorities and Chinese landlords, and had no state to protect their interests. Kang Gyeongae's fiction documents this world from the inside — not as exotic or romantic migration, but as the everyday material reality of extreme poverty and displacement.

About the Text

Salt and Survival

Salt (소금, Sogŭm) follows a Korean woman in the Manchurian diaspora struggling to survive under extreme poverty. The story belongs to Kang's larger project of documenting the lives of the most marginalized — poor Korean women in Manchuria who existed at the intersection of colonial displacement, class exploitation, and gender subordination. These are women who are not only poor but displaced, not only displaced but women, and for whom the colonial and patriarchal systems overlap and reinforce each other.

Salt is, at its most basic level, a preservative — something that keeps things from decaying, that makes survival possible. It is also a bare necessity, something too fundamental to be luxury. The title's resonance suggests both the minimal conditions of survival and the woman's own status: essential, necessary, undervalued, taken for granted.

Socialist Realism and Gender

Kang Gyeongae is one of the few colonial-era writers who wrote explicitly about class and gender simultaneously, rather than treating them as separate concerns. Her socialist realism is attentive to the specific ways that colonial capitalism produces gendered suffering: the women in her fiction are poor because of class, displaced because of colonialism, and exploited in specifically gendered ways because of patriarchy. These are not parallel oppressions but interlocking ones.

This distinguishes her from both the New Woman writers (Na Hyesŏk, Kim Wonju), who focused on educated elite women's autonomy, and from the male naturalists (Kim Tongin), who wrote about poor women as objects of deterministic fate without asking about the social production of their situation. Kang Gyeongae wrote about poor women as subjects of their own suffering — people with a specific analysis of their condition, not just victims of it.

Key Concepts

Gando / Jiandao (간도)
The region in northeast China (now Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture, Jilin Province) where hundreds of thousands of Koreans migrated during the Japanese colonial period. Subject of the 1909 Gando Convention between Japan and China, which established Chinese sovereignty over the region while guaranteeing certain rights for Korean residents. The Korean community in Gando maintained Korean language, culture, and political organizations.
Proletarian literature (프롤레타리아 문학)
A Korean literary movement of the 1920s–30s associated with the Korea Artista Proleta Federatio (KAPF, 카프), which argued that literature should serve the class struggle. Kang Gyeongae was associated with this tradition, though her work transcends its more programmatic expressions through its specificity and its attention to gender.
Intersectionality in colonial context
The concept of intersecting oppressions — race, class, gender — was not available to Kang Gyeongae as a theoretical framework, but her fiction operates as a practice of intersectional analysis avant la lettre. She writes about women who are simultaneously Korean (colonial subjects), poor (class-exploited), and women (gender-subordinated) in ways that resist reduction to any single axis of oppression.

Discussion Questions

1. Kang Gyeongae situates this story in Manchuria, not Korea proper. What does this geographical displacement add to the story's analysis of colonial life?
2. Compare Kang Gyeongae's socialist realism with Kim Tongin's naturalism in "Potatoes." Both portray poor women in colonial Korea — how does the political framework shape the narrative differently?
3. Kang Gyeongae is one of the few colonial-era women writers who wrote explicitly about class as well as gender. How do race, class, and gender intersect in Salt?
4. Kang died before liberation. How does her early death shape how we read her work and her place in the Korean literary canon?
5. This story is placed in Week 7 alongside Yū Miri's Full House — both deal with Koreans outside Korea proper. What do the two texts share, and how do they differ?
