Deep Dive: Court Memoir — Week 1

Hanjungnok

한중록  ·  Lady Hyegyeong
Lady Hyegyeong (헌경왕후 홍씨, 1735–1815) Written 1795–1805 Court Memoir (궁중 회고록) Joseon Dynasty

About the Author

Crown Princess Consort of Joseon

Lady Hyegyeong was born Hong Ponghan's daughter in 1735 and entered the palace at age nine as the chosen bride of Crown Prince Sado (사도세자). She lived through one of the most traumatic events in the Joseon dynasty: the execution of her own husband in 1762 by her father-in-law, King Yeongjo, who ordered Sado confined inside a rice storage chest (뒤주) where he died over eight days.

She survived by navigating the palace's violent factional politics for decades afterward, outliving her husband, her son King Jeongjo, and ultimately witnessing the accession of her grandson. Her memoirs were the principal act of her later life — a legal and literary effort to fix the historical record before she died.

Writing Across Four Installments

The Hanjungnok was not written as a single work. Lady Hyegyeong composed four separate installments between 1795 and 1805, each with a different rhetorical purpose. The first memoir (1795) was written for her nephew; subsequent memoirs addressed her son King Jeongjo, a later nephew, and finally her grandson King Sunjo. Each installment had to calibrate its account of the past to suit the political needs of the present. The result is a text that speaks in multiple voices and must be read as a political document as well as a personal one.

The title Hanjungnok (한중록) is sometimes translated as Memoirs Written in Silence or The Memoir of 1805. JaHyun Kim Haboush's English translation, published by Columbia University Press in 1996, is the standard scholarly edition and the version assigned in this course.

Historical Context

The Execution of Crown Prince Sado, 1762

The execution of Crown Prince Sado was not an aberration in Joseon history — it was the most extreme expression of ongoing factional conflicts between the Noron (노론) and Soron (소론) parties that defined late Joseon politics. The prince had displayed erratic, violent behavior for years; the historical record shows he killed palace servants and may have been severely mentally ill. But the decision to execute him was also a political maneuver: King Yeongjo needed to protect the throne line while eliminating a political liability.

King Yeongjo's chosen method — the rice chest — was designed specifically to avoid the formal taboo against shedding royal blood directly. This legalistic cruelty is itself revealing of how Joseon's ritual system operated even at its most violent moments. Lady Hyegyeong was present throughout the events of July 4, 1762, and her account remains the only firsthand testimony.

Factional Politics: Noron and Soron

Lady Hyegyeong's family, the Andong Hong clan, were Noron (노론) partisans. Crown Prince Sado was associated with the Soron faction and with a circle of officials critical of the Noron. The execution therefore had factional as well as personal dimensions: eliminating Sado removed a potential patron for Noron rivals. Lady Hyegyeong's subsequent memoirs were partly a sustained argument that her own family bore no responsibility for Sado's death — an argument with high political stakes for her son King Jeongjo, who publicly mourned his father while needing to work with the Noron court that had helped destroy him.

Reading the Hanjungnok requires understanding that every claim it makes is also a political position in an ongoing factional struggle. Lady Hyegyeong is never simply a grieving widow. She is always also an advocate.

The Mother-Son Bond: Lady Hyegyeong and King Jeongjo

King Jeongjo (r. 1776–1800) was perhaps the most capable king of the late Joseon period — a reforming monarch who built the Hwaseong Fortress partly to honor his father Sado's memory. The relationship between Jeongjo and his mother is one of the emotional centers of the memoirs. Lady Hyegyeong writes with evident pride and affection for her son, but also with anxiety: her political survival depended on his, and he died before her, in 1800 under circumstances that some historians consider suspicious.

The Throne (2015), a South Korean film also assigned in this course, dramatizes the events of 1762. Students should compare the film's characterizations of Yeongjo and Sado with the memoir's account. Lady Hyegyeong's Yeongjo is more frightening and more complex than most film portrayals allow.

The Text as Literature

Hangul Prose and Women's Literary Voice

Lady Hyegyeong wrote in Hangul, the Korean phonetic script developed in the fifteenth century, rather than in classical Chinese (hanmun), the prestige written language of Joseon's male elite. This choice was not a sign of limited education — Lady Hyegyeong was clearly highly literate — but a deliberate decision about audience and register. Hangul writing in the Joseon period was associated with women, with domestic and emotional expression, and with accessibility. By writing in Hangul, she was positioning her memoir as personal testimony rather than official history.

This matters because official history was controlled by men in the Joseon bureaucracy. Lady Hyegyeong could not enter that record directly. The memoir is her mechanism for shaping how events would be remembered by those who could — her own descendants. Haboush's translation preserves the formal complexity of the Korean original, which moves between registers of grief, legal argument, and political address in ways that reward close reading.

Key Figures

Crown Prince Sado (사도세자, 1735–1762)
Lady Hyegyeong's husband, executed by his father King Yeongjo. The posthumous name "Sado" (思悼, "mourning in thought") was given by Yeongjo himself. The prince's violent behavior and mental deterioration before his death are documented in multiple sources, though the causes remain debated.
King Yeongjo (영조, r. 1724–1776)
The longest-reigning Joseon king, credited with administrative reforms and the famous "Tangpyeong" (탕평) policy of factional balance. Also the king who killed his own son. The Hanjungnok's portrait of Yeongjo is the most intimate and disturbing we have.
King Jeongjo (정조, r. 1776–1800)
Lady Hyegyeong's son, one of the Joseon dynasty's most celebrated reforming kings. Builder of Hwaseong Fortress, champion of practical learning (silhak). His death in 1800 preceded his mother's by fifteen years.
Noron / Soron (노론 / 소론)
The two dominant factional blocs of late Joseon, splits within the Westerner (Sŏin) faction. The Noron backed Yeongjo's succession; the Soron backed his rivals. Lady Hyegyeong's family were Noron.

Discussion Questions

1. How does Lady Hyegyeong use her position as eyewitness to construct authority? What are the limits of her perspective as an interested party in the events she describes?
2. What does the text reveal about women's power and powerlessness within the Joseon court? In what ways does Lady Hyegyeong exercise agency, and where is she constrained?
3. The memoir was written in Hangul, not classical Chinese. What does this choice signal about audience and intent?
4. How does Lady Hyegyeong navigate loyalty to her husband versus loyalty to her king (her father-in-law)? Is this a genuine ethical dilemma, or does she resolve it too easily?
5. The Throne (2015) dramatizes the same events. Compare the film's portrayal of Yeongjo and Sado with the memoir's perspective. What does each medium allow or foreclose?
