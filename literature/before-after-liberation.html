Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 3

Before and After Liberation

해방전후 (Haebang jŏnhu)  ·  Yi Taejun
Yi Taejun (이태준, 1904–?) 1946 Short story / autobiographical fiction Liberation era

About the Author

The Prose Stylist Who Went North

Yi Taejun (이태준) is widely regarded as one of the finest prose stylists in modern Korean literature — a writer whose sentences are valued for their precision, warmth, and lyrical restraint. Born in 1904 in North Hamgyŏng Province (now North Korea), he came to Seoul and built a literary career as a short story writer, essayist, and literary organizer. He was a founding member of Kuinhoe (구인회, "The Group of Nine"), a literary circle that also included Yi Sang and included prominent modernist and realist writers.

His fate after 1946 is one of the defining tragedies of Korean literary history. He moved to North Korea in 1946 — a decision made at the moment of maximum political possibility and uncertainty, when the peninsula had not yet formally divided and many Korean intellectuals believed the North offered better prospects for a genuinely independent Korea. In North Korea he wrote under the DPRK literary system, but fell out of favor after 1956 during the purges following the Korean Workers' Party political struggles. His fate after that is unknown. In South Korea, his works were banned for decades because of his move to the North.

A Banned Writer Recovered

Yi Taejun's works were banned in South Korea from the 1950s until the late 1980s — a period spanning most of the authoritarian era — because he had gone North. This meant that one of the finest Korean prose writers of the twentieth century was effectively erased from the South Korean literary canon for thirty years. His rehabilitation after the democratization of the late 1980s was a significant cultural event, and reading his work now requires awareness of this double history: the story's own meaning, and the history of its suppression and recovery.

This is a recurring pattern in the literature of divided Korea. The political division produced a literary division in which writers on the wrong side of the line were simply deleted. Yi Taejun is perhaps the most significant casualty of this erasure, though he is far from alone.

About the Text

The Euphoria and Fracturing of 1945

"Before and After Liberation" is a semi-autobiographical story that follows an intellectual writer — clearly a stand-in for Yi Taejun himself — navigating the transition from Japanese colonial rule to liberation in August 1945. The story captures the specific texture of that moment: the sudden collapse of the colonial order, the disorientation of people who had organized their lives around its constraints, the explosion of political possibility and the simultaneous awareness that the possible futures were going to be bitterly contested.

Liberation in the story is not simply joy. It is joy mixed with confusion, with fear about what comes next, and with the dawning awareness that the political divisions among Koreans — right and left, nationalist and communist, northern and southern — were not going to be resolved by the departure of the Japanese. The peninsula was already fracturing, and the story registers this fracture at the level of individual consciousness.

The Intellectual and Political Commitment

The story's narrator faces a question that was existential for Korean intellectuals in 1945: where do you stand? The political left and right both demanded commitment. The American occupation government in the South had its own requirements. Various Korean nationalist factions had their own litmus tests. The story explores the difficulty of maintaining independent intellectual judgment in a moment when everyone demands that you choose a side.

Yi Taejun himself chose — he went North. Reading the story against this biographical fact is tempting, but it risks over-determining the text. The story's value is not as autobiography but as a precise historical record of what it felt like to stand at that particular crossroads, not yet knowing which way to go.

Key Concepts

Kuinhoe (구인회, "The Group of Nine")
A literary circle founded in Seoul in 1933, including Yi Taejun, Yi Sang, and several other major writers. Associated with a modernist aesthetic that emphasized craft over ideological commitment — a position that was itself a kind of political stance in the colonial context.
The 1945–48 transition period
The years between Japan's surrender (August 1945) and the formal establishment of the Republic of Korea and DPRK (both 1948) were a period of intense political contestation. The US military occupied the South; the Soviet military the North. Korean factions of all kinds competed to shape the new political order. Violence was common.
Division of the literary canon
The political division of Korea produced a literary division. South Korea banned works by writers who went North; North Korea's literary establishment condemned and erased writers who stayed South or fled. Yi Taejun's rehabilitation in South Korea is part of a broader post-democratization recovery of the divided literary tradition.

Discussion Questions

1. Yi Taejun moved to North Korea after writing this story. Does knowing this change how you read the narrator's political hesitations?
2. How does the story represent the experience of liberation — as joy, uncertainty, or something more complicated?
3. What pressures do intellectuals face in the story? How do they navigate political demands on their writing?
4. Compare this story's portrait of 1945 with Han Sorya's "Jackals." How does ideological position shape literary form?
5. Yi Taejun's works were banned in South Korea for decades. What does his censorship history reveal about how states control literary memory?
