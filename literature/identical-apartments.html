Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 5

Identical Apartments

닮은 방들  ·  Pak Wanso
Pak Wanso (박완서, 1931–2011) 1974 Short Story South Korea

About the Author

Pak Wanso and the 1970s

Pak Wanso (박완서) published her first novel in 1970 and quickly became one of the most widely read and respected writers in South Korea. The 1970s were her most politically charged decade: Park Chung-hee's Yusin system (1972–79) imposed authoritarian rule alongside an economic modernization drive that transformed Seoul with breakneck speed. New industrial zones, new highways, and above all: new apartment complexes, rising across the city as the government pursued its vision of Korean development.

Pak Wanso watched this transformation with a sharp and critical eye. Her stories of the 1970s document what the "economic miracle" did to the texture of domestic life — particularly to women's lives, which were shaped by and confined within the new domestic spaces that development created. See also The Day My Housewatching Ended (also Week 5), another story from this period.

About the Text

What Happens in the Story

The narrator is a married daughter living in a large apartment crowded with her husband's extended family. Her central irritant is something small but precise: she resents having to listen for her husband's doorbell ring, unable to answer the door herself because the apartment belongs to in-laws. The resentment is petty, and she knows it. That self-awareness doesn't dissolve it.

When the family finally moves to their own unit, she befriends a neighbor and begins, almost without noticing, to copy her — her cooking, her style, her habits. The move that was supposed to free her from identical living replicates it at one remove. Her resentment toward the doorbell "conveniently morphs into a hatred of the peephole." She has grown to hate everything for which there is a duplicate. The story ends with a plot turn that crystallizes the central irony: her apartment, her life, her psychology has become identical to everything around her, and she cannot locate an exit from that condition.

What "Identical" Means

The title operates on multiple levels, and the story earns each of them. The apartments are physically identical — same layout, same finishes, same floor plan in every building of the complex. But the story's real subject is the way the woman's desires become identical: the copied neighbor, the morphing resentments, the reflexive unhappiness that attaches to whatever target is nearest. She does not lose herself through dramatic erasure. She loses herself through a series of small substitutions, each one barely perceptible.

One reviewer read the protagonist's relentless dissatisfaction as clinical rather than political. Pak Wanso's point is that under the regime of identical apartments, the two are not distinguishable. The environment that produces conformity also produces the psychology that can't escape it. The doorbell and the peephole are the same object in different apartments.

Domestic Critique as Political Speech

Under the Yusin system's censorship regime, direct political critique was dangerous. Writing about women's domestic experience and the alienation of apartment life was, on the surface, apolitical. But Pak Wanso's domestic focus was never innocent of political implication. The apartment is the product of the Park Chung-hee development regime; the women confined in it are confined by the same social norms that regime required to mobilize male labor and capital. Critiquing the apartment is critiquing the system that built it, even if that critique never uses political vocabulary.

This indirection is not timidity. It is a literary strategy that allows the critique to operate in the space between what is said and what the reader understands.

Key Concepts

Apateu (아파트) culture
The South Korean apartment complex — standardized, high-density, government-promoted — became the dominant form of urban housing from the 1970s onward. Moving into an apateu was associated with middle-class status and modernity. The uniformity of these spaces became a recurring subject in Korean literature and film.
Han'gang ŭi kijŏk (한강의 기적, "Miracle on the Han")
The term for South Korea's rapid postwar economic development, especially in the 1960s–80s under Park Chung-hee. Real GDP grew at roughly 10% per year. The "miracle" required intensive mobilization of cheap labor, the suppression of labor rights, and massive state direction of capital — all of which had gendered costs that writers like Pak Wanso documented.
Women's domestic labor (가사노동)
Korean development ideology in the 1960s–70s constructed women's domestic labor as the invisible foundation of national development: wives managing households, raising children to enter the labor force, supporting husbands' productive work. This construction made women's domestic work simultaneously essential and undervalued.

Discussion Questions

1. What does "identical" mean in this story beyond the physical apartments? What else is being made identical?
2. How does Pak Wanso connect urban space to women's psychology and social constraint?
3. The 1970s apartment boom reshaped Seoul radically. How does the story register this transformation — as progress, loss, or something else?
4. Compare Pak Wanso's domestic critique with the political critique in Kim Jiha's "Five Bandits." Can domestic alienation be read as political resistance?
5. Pak Wanso was writing under political censorship. How might the domestic focus of this story be a way of speaking politically while appearing apolitical?
