Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 4

A Pasque-Flower on That Bleak Day

할미꽃  ·  Pak Wanso
Pak Wanso (박완서, 1931–2011) 1977 Short Story South Korea

About the Author

The Late-Starting Giant

Pak Wanso (박완서) is one of the most important figures in modern Korean literature, and her life story is itself remarkable. She was born in 1931 in Kyŏnggi Province and survived the Korean War working for the American military in Seoul — an experience that gave her material she would draw on for decades. She did not publish her first novel until she was 40 years old, when Namokhŏn (나목, "Bare Tree," 1970) appeared in a literary competition. From that late beginning she went on to produce dozens of novels and story collections, winning every major Korean literary prize and earning a readership that spanned generations.

Her subject throughout was the texture of women's lives in postwar Korea: the Korean War and its long aftermath, the rapid industrialization and urbanization of the 1960s–70s, the domestic sphere and its relationship to the larger social world, class mobility and its costs. She wrote with unflinching clarity and remarkable range — from comic domestic observation to harrowing accounts of wartime violence. She died in 2011.

The Korean War Through Women's Eyes

Korean War literature in South Korea has been dominated by men writing about combat. Pak Wanso wrote about the war from a different vantage point: what it did to women, to families, to the fabric of ordinary life. Her war is not the battlefield but the ruins of Seoul after the armies had passed through — the occupation, survival, loss, and the weight of memory that would not lift for decades.

Her first novel, Namokhŏn, is autobiographical: set in Seoul during the Korean War, centered on a woman working for the American military who falls into a relationship she cannot afford emotionally. "A Pasque-Flower on That Bleak Day" continues this project: war memory surfacing in the present, refusing to stay in the past where survivors need it to stay.

About the Text

The Grandmother Flower

The pasque-flower (할미꽃, halmikkot) is sometimes called the "grandmother flower" in Korean — a small, bent flower that blooms close to the ground, associated in folk tradition with elderly women and with mortality. The plant's shape suggests a stooped old woman; it blooms in early spring, often in rocky or impoverished soil. By choosing this image as the story's title, Pak Wanso positions it immediately in the register of old women, endurance, and the beautiful-despite-hardship.

The story uses this image to explore the relationship between personal memory and collective history. The war is decades past; the story is set in the comfortable present of 1977 South Korea. But the war is not past for the story's protagonist. It surfaces in unexpected moments, in images that cannot be put away, in a sense of guilt and loss that the prosperity of the present has not resolved.

Writing Under Park Chung-hee

Pak Wanso published this story in 1977, at the height of Park Chung-hee's Yusin (유신) authoritarian system. Direct political critique was dangerous. Literary censors monitored publications for content that could be construed as anti-government, pro-Communist, or destabilizing. Within these constraints, writers developed strategies for saying political things in forms that appeared apolitical.

Pak Wanso's focus on domestic and personal life — on war memory as a private experience, on women's inner worlds — was not simply a retreat from politics. It was a way of keeping alive an account of the Korean War and its costs that the official state narrative suppressed: not the heroic war of anti-Communist defense, but the war as devastation, loss, and ongoing wound. The personal is, in this context, unavoidably political.

Key Concepts

Pasque-flower (할미꽃)
The halmikkot ("grandmother flower") is a Korean wildflower associated in folk tradition with old women and endurance. It blooms in early spring, often in poor soil. Pak Wanso's choice of this image positions the story at the intersection of women, aging, survival, and beauty-despite-hardship.
Namokhŏn (나목, 1970)
Pak Wanso's debut novel, set during the Korean War in Seoul. Semi-autobiographical: a woman works for the US military, navigates wartime survival, and observes a painter named Park Soo-geun. Established the Korean War and women's perspective as the central subjects of her career.
Yusin (유신) system (1972–79)
Park Chung-hee's authoritarian "revitalizing reform" constitution of 1972, which concentrated power in the presidency, eliminated direct presidential elections, and imposed emergency decrees criminalizing political dissent. Literary and cultural censorship intensified during this period.

Discussion Questions

1. Pak Wanso is famous for portraying what war does to women's lives — not the battlefield but the aftermath. What specific forms does that aftermath take in this story?
2. The pasque-flower (할미꽃) is the "grandmother flower" — an old, bent flower. How does this image work in the story?
3. How does the story connect personal memory to national history?
4. Compare this story's war memory with "Cranes." How do gender and perspective shape each author's treatment of the Korean War?
5. Pak Wanso published this story in 1977, under the Park Chung-hee dictatorship. How does political context shape what can and cannot be said in the story?
