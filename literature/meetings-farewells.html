Deep Dive: Short Story — Week 2

Meetings and Farewells

봉별기 (Bongbyŏlgi)  ·  Yi Sang
Yi Sang (이상, 1910–1937) 1936 Short story / autobiographical fiction Colonial Korea

About the Author

Korea's Great Modernist, Dead at 26

Yi Sang (이상) was the pen name of Kim Hae-gyŏng (김해경), born in 1910 in Seoul. His trajectory was as compressed as it was brilliant: he trained as an architect at the Government-General Technical School, worked briefly for the Japanese colonial government's engineering bureau, developed tuberculosis, opened a tearoom in Jongno called "제비" (Swallow), wrote ferociously, and died in 1937 in a Tokyo prison, arrested on vague suspicion of anti-Japanese thought crimes. He was 26.

His literary output in those few years was astonishing in its formal ambition. His poetry uses mathematical notation, typographical experiment, and stream-of-consciousness in ways that had no precedent in Korean literature. His prose fiction — particularly the story "Wings" (날개, 1936) and "Meetings and Farewells" — combines radical subjectivity with precise observation of colonial Seoul's modernity. He remains the most discussed and most difficult figure in the modern Korean literary canon.

The Late-Colonial Seoul Modernist

Yi Sang's Seoul was a city being transformed at speed by colonial capital: department stores, tearooms, cinemas, electric lights, Western-style cafes, bar hostesses. This was the world of the modŏn pŏi (모던 보이, "modern boy") and modŏn gŏl (모던 걸, "modern girl") — Koreans performing new urbanized modern identities under the sign of a colonialism that was simultaneously developing and exploiting the city. Yi Sang's fiction inhabits this world with absolute intimacy and profound unease. The neon glow and the tuberculosis are inseparable.

About the Text

Bongbyŏlgi: A Record of Meetings and Farewells

The title 봉별기 (Bongbyŏlgi) can be rendered "a record of meetings and farewells" — bong meaning to meet, byŏl meaning to part, gi a record or memoir. The story follows the narrator's relationship with Gŭm-hong (금홍), a bar hostess he meets and becomes entangled with, in the specific emotional and economic texture of late-colonial Seoul's entertainment district. The relationship is not conventionally romantic; it is shot through with desire, money, illness, and the narrator's relentless self-analysis.

Unlike Yi Sang's most formally experimental work, "Meetings and Farewells" is relatively accessible in narrative terms — it has a discernible story, identifiable characters, a beginning and an end. But it is still unmistakably Yi Sang: the narrator's interiority dominates everything, his self-consciousness is so complete that it threatens to consume what he is actually observing, and the emotional register shifts without warning between tenderness, detachment, and something close to despair.

The Unreliable Narrator and the Bar Hostess

One of the most productive questions the story raises is what we know about Gŭm-hong. The answer is: very little that we have not received filtered through a narrator who is obsessively self-focused and almost certainly not reliable. Gŭm-hong is present in the story as an object of desire, as a puzzle the narrator cannot solve, as a reflection of his own anxieties about illness and mortality. She acts, she speaks, she has opinions — but they are all mediated through a narrator who is not equipped to understand anyone but himself.

This is not a failure of the story but its subject. Yi Sang's modernism is interested in the limits of the self as an instrument of knowledge. The narrator cannot see Gŭm-hong clearly because subjectivity is not transparent to itself, let alone to others. The reader, standing outside both of them, can see the gap — and that gap is where the story's meaning lives.

Tuberculosis, Mortality, and Modernist Form

Yi Sang had tuberculosis when he wrote this story, and the awareness of a shortened life permeates his work in a way that is difficult to separate from its formal choices. The radical compression, the refusal of conventional narrative arc, the emphasis on the present moment's texture — these are literary strategies but they are also the strategies of someone who knows time is running out. Reading Yi Sang without this biographical context is possible; reading him with it adds a layer of resonance that the text does not reject.

Gŭm-hong too is marked by the logic of the temporary — their relationship is structured by meetings and farewells, by the awareness that it is contingent, fragile, unlikely to survive. The title's insistence on both encounter and departure holds together the story's emotional poles: the desire to connect and the knowledge that connection does not last.

Key Concepts

Modŏn pŏi / modŏn gŏl (모던 보이 / 모던 걸)
"Modern boy" / "modern girl" — colonial-era Korean figures performing urbanized modern identity through fashion, leisure, and new consumer culture. Associated with the Jongno entertainment district and the tearooms, bars, and cafes of late-colonial Seoul.
Bar hostess (카페 여급)
A category of female labor in colonial Korea's new entertainment economy: women who worked in cafes and bars, providing conversation and companionship to male customers. The social and economic conditions of this labor are a recurring subject in colonial-era fiction.
Korean modernism
A literary movement of the 1930s influenced by Western and Japanese avant-garde forms — stream-of-consciousness, surrealism, Dadaism, typographical experiment. Yi Sang is its defining figure. Korean modernism must be understood in the colonial context: formal experimentation was one way to write under censorship without making explicit political claims.

Discussion Questions

1. Yi Sang's narrator is unreliable and self-obsessed. How does this affect your reading of Gŭm-hong's perspective? What can you recover of her that the narrator cannot see?
2. How does the story represent colonial Seoul as a space of both excitement and dread?
3. Yi Sang died at 26. How does the awareness of mortality shape the story's emotional texture?
4. Compare the female characters in this story with those in "Kyŏnghŭi" and "Potatoes." How does class shape each woman's situation and representation?
5. Yi Sang's modernism was influenced by Western and Japanese avant-garde movements. What is distinctly Korean about his sensibility?
