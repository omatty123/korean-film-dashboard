Deep Dive: Satirical Poem — Week 6

Five Bandits

오적 (O Jeok)  ·  Kim Jiha
Kim Jiha (김지하, 1941–2022) 1970 Satirical poem (pansori-style) South Korea

About the Author

South Korea's Dissident Poet

Kim Jiha (김지하) was born Kim Yŏng-il (김영일) in 1941 in South Chŏlla Province. He became the defining poet of South Korea's pro-democracy movement under the Park Chung-hee regime — a figure whose arrests, torture, and death sentences made him a cause célèbre internationally in the 1970s, with Amnesty International and international writers campaigning for his release. He was imprisoned multiple times, spent years in solitary confinement, and in 1974 was sentenced to death under the Anti-Communist Law, later commuted under international pressure.

After his release in the late 1970s, Kim Jiha became increasingly associated with Catholic social teaching and minjung (민중) theology — a Korean liberation theology that identified the suffering poor as the locus of God's presence in history. His later career was more controversial: in the 1990s he issued statements that critics read as attacking the very democratic movement he had helped build, generating debate about his legacy that has not been resolved.

The Arrest and the Poem's Significance

"Five Bandits" (오적, O Jeok) was published in the journal Sasanggye (사상계, "World of Thought") in May 1970. Kim Jiha was arrested the following month under the Anti-Communist Law. The speed of the arrest tells you everything about how the Park Chung-hee government read the poem: as a direct and dangerous threat, not a literary exercise.

The state's overreaction was itself a measure of the poem's power. Kim Jiha had found a form — traditional pansori performance poetry, beloved by ordinary Koreans — and filled it with a devastating satire of the very ruling class that the regime was supposed to protect and advance. The poem circulated in samizdat copies throughout Korea, read aloud at protest gatherings, performing the function of political speech precisely because it was framed as poetry.

About the Text

Pansori Form and Political Satire

Pansori (판소리) is a traditional Korean performance art: a single vocalist and a drummer, performing long narrative poems that mix tragedy, comedy, social observation, and melodrama. It originated in the Joseon period and is associated with the common people — the farmers, merchants, and artisans who were its original audience. The form is earthy, explicit, funny, and capable of extraordinary emotional range.

By writing "Five Bandits" in pansori style, Kim Jiha made three simultaneous moves. He claimed a popular, vernacular Korean tradition against the regime's modernization rhetoric. He used the form's humor and excess to say things that were undeniable even to those who wanted to deny them. And he ensured that the poem could be performed — read aloud with the rhythms and energy of the original tradition — rather than merely consumed as a printed text. The form was the political argument as much as the content.

Who Are the Five Bandits?

The five bandits of the poem are: the chaebol (재벌, the large business conglomerates); members of the National Assembly; high government officials; generals; and cabinet ministers. This is a systematic analysis of where power and corruption reside in Park Chung-hee's South Korea. Kim Jiha is not attacking individuals but structural positions — the organized corruption of a system in which capital and state power have fused to extract wealth from ordinary Koreans.

The poem's portrait of the chaebol is particularly significant given how central the chaebol system became to South Korean economic development and how normalized its corruption became. Kim Jiha identified this corruption as morally catastrophic in 1970; the system he attacked has evolved but not disappeared, as any Korean can tell you.

Minjung (민중) and the People

The concept of minjung (민중, "the people" or "the masses") became central to South Korean pro-democracy thinking and theology in the 1970s–80s. It refers not just to "the people" in the abstract but to the specific Korean people who suffer under economic exploitation and political repression — farmers, industrial workers, urban poor. Minjung theology, developed by Korean Protestant and Catholic thinkers, identified this suffering people with the biblical poor and argued that working for their liberation was the core of Christian ethics.

Kim Jiha's poem belongs to this tradition even before the formal minjung theology movement took shape. Its target — the five bandits who exploit the minjung — and its form — the people's own art form — are both expressions of this political and spiritual commitment.

Key Concepts

Pansori (판소리)
Traditional Korean vocal performance art: one singer and one drummer, performing long narrative poems that combine tragedy, comedy, social satire, and melodrama. Associated with the common people. Kim Jiha's deployment of this form for political satire was a deliberate claim of popular cultural tradition against the modernizing state.
Chaebol (재벌)
South Korean large conglomerates — Samsung, Hyundai, LG, SK, etc. — built through close state-business collaboration under Park Chung-hee. Kim Jiha identified the chaebol as one of the "five bandits" extracting wealth from ordinary Koreans. The chaebol system's corruption scandals have continued into the 21st century.
Minjung (민중)
The Korean concept of "the people" as specifically the exploited and oppressed majority — farmers, workers, urban poor. Minjung theology (민중신학) developed in the 1970s identified this people with the biblical poor and became the spiritual framework for much of the pro-democracy movement.
Anti-Communist Law (반공법)
South Korean law used extensively under Park Chung-hee to prosecute activists, intellectuals, and writers. Kim Jiha was arrested under this law after publishing "Five Bandits." The law criminalized any speech or action that could be construed as benefiting communism — a category interpreted broadly enough to include political satire.

Discussion Questions

1. Kim Jiha chose pansori — a traditional popular performance form — for a radical political poem. What does this formal choice accomplish?
2. The five "bandits" are all powerful figures in the state and economic system. What does this suggest about Kim Jiha's theory of where corruption originates?
3. Kim Jiha was arrested for this poem. What does that tell us about the power that authoritarian governments ascribe to literature?
4. Compare this poem's approach to political critique with Chae Mansik's satirical short story "Constable Maeng." How do the two satirists work differently across their chosen forms?
5. Kim Jiha's later career was controversial — he moved away from the left. Does this change how you read "Five Bandits"?
