Deep Dive: Short story / Anthology excerpt — Week 7

Full House

풀 하우스  ·  Yū Miri (유미리)
Yū Miri (유미리, born 1968) 1995 Short story / play excerpt Zainichi Korean / Japan

About the Author

Zainichi Korean Writer, Akutagawa Prize Laureate

Yū Miri (유미리) was born in 1968 in Yokohama, Japan. She is Zainichi Korean (재일 조선인) — an ethnic Korean born and raised in Japan, a member of the community that is one of the largest ethnic minority groups in Japanese society. She became a playwright first, winning the Kishida Drama Prize for her theater work, before transitioning to fiction. In 1997 she won the Akutagawa Prize — Japan's most prestigious literary award — for Family Cinema (家族シネマ), one of the few times the prize has gone to a Zainichi Korean writer.

Her work explores family dysfunction, trauma, memory, and the experience of being Korean in Japan — of belonging to a community that is culturally Korean in significant ways but socially and linguistically Japanese, and that occupies an uncomfortable position in both societies. In 2015, she made a dramatic personal choice: she moved to the town of Namie in Fukushima Prefecture (near the site of the 2011 nuclear disaster), and has since become a prominent advocate for the region's recovery. She has also associated herself publicly with North Korea, reflecting her sense of identification with Korean-ness in its most politically charged form.

What Is Zainichi Korean?

Zainichi Koreans (재일 조선인 or 재일 한국인) are ethnic Koreans living in Japan, most of whom are descendants of Koreans brought to Japan during the colonial period (1910–1945) as forced laborers or who migrated for economic reasons. The community numbers roughly 500,000–600,000 people. They are one of the most consequential ethnic minorities in Japan, with a complex history that includes significant discrimination, violence (notably during the 1923 Great Kantō Earthquake, when thousands of Koreans were massacred in Tokyo), and ongoing legal and social challenges.

Many Zainichi Koreans hold Korean nationality (either South or North, depending on family history and registration choices) despite never having lived in Korea. They face discrimination in employment and housing in Japan, complex questions about assimilation versus ethnic identity preservation, and difficult choices about national registration. The question of what it means to be Korean in Japan — to speak Japanese as a native language, to have never lived in Korea, to be considered "Japanese" by Koreans and "Korean" by Japanese — is the central condition of Zainichi Korean literature.

About the Text

Into the Light: Zainichi Korean Literature in English

Full House appears in Into the Light: An Anthology of Literature by Koreans in Japan (ed. Melissa L. Wender, University of Hawai'i Press, 2011), a collection that brought Zainichi Korean literature to English-language readers for the first time in a substantial anthology. Wender's introduction to the anthology is itself essential reading for understanding the context of Zainichi literature: what it means to write in Japanese about being Korean, what the literary tradition looks like from the inside, and why it has been largely invisible to both Korean and Japanese literary canons.

Yū Miri's contribution to that anthology — Full House — is drawn from her early career as a playwright and reflects her theatrical background. It explores the Zainichi Korean experience through the domestic space of a household: the "full house" of the title may be full of family, of memory, of the weight of colonial history that the family carries without necessarily naming.

Language, Identity, and the Zainichi Condition

One of the most striking aspects of Zainichi Korean literature is its linguistic situation. Yū Miri writes in Japanese — not as a second language, but as her native language. She did not grow up speaking Korean, though she has connections to Korean cultural identity. This means she is a Korean writer whose literature is Japanese literature: available to Japanese readers, evaluated by Japanese critics, winning Japanese prizes. But she writes about being Korean in ways that Japanese literature produced by ethnic Japanese does not and cannot.

This situation — which is the condition of a diasporic writer in a former colonial metropole — generates specific kinds of literary consciousness: a hypersensitivity to the gap between social identity and ethnic identity, a particular relationship to language as both connection and barrier, an awareness of how much one's social existence depends on the choices of larger political forces that one did not make and cannot control. Yū Miri's work inhabits this condition from inside.

The Question of Home

For Zainichi Koreans, the question of "home" is radically open. Japan is where they were born and live; Korea is where their family heritage points. But "Korea" itself is divided — and for Zainichi Koreans who identify with North Korea (as many of the older generation did, through the organization Chongryun/Chongryon), even the "homeland" is a state they cannot visit. Yū Miri's move to Fukushima — near the DMZ of Japanese public life, near catastrophe — and her identification with the DPRK are extreme versions of a question that every Zainichi Korean faces: where is home?

Read alongside Kang Gyeongae's Salt, both texts examine Koreans outside Korea proper, asking what belonging means when the political reality has made belonging impossible to complete.

Key Concepts

Zainichi Korean (재일 조선인 / 재일 한국인)
Ethnic Koreans permanently residing in Japan. Most are descendants of Koreans who came to Japan during the colonial period. "Zainichi" (在日) literally means "residing in Japan." The community's legal status, language situation, and cultural identity are extremely complex. Discrimination in Japan, and cultural distance from both South and North Korea, define their position.
Chongryun / Chongryon (총련)
The General Association of Korean Residents in Japan — the pro-North Korea organization that historically organized much of the Zainichi Korean community, especially older generations. Members registered as North Korean nationals. The organization ran Korean schools, community centers, and social services. Its influence has declined significantly since the 1990s.
Akutagawa Prize (芥川龍之介賞)
Japan's most prestigious literary prize for new fiction, given twice yearly since 1935. Winning the Akutagawa Prize places a writer at the center of Japanese literary culture. Yū Miri's 1997 win was a landmark moment for Zainichi Korean literature's recognition within Japan.
Colonial-era Korean migration to Japan
During the colonial period (1910–1945), hundreds of thousands of Koreans were brought to Japan as forced laborers, or migrated voluntarily for economic reasons. After liberation, many chose to remain in Japan (or were unable to return). They and their descendants form the current Zainichi community. Japan has never formally apologized for the forced migration of colonial-era workers.

Discussion Questions

1. Yū Miri writes in Japanese about being Korean. What does this linguistic situation reveal about Zainichi Korean identity?
2. Compare the Zainichi Korean experience in Japan with the Korean diaspora in Manchuria depicted in Kang Gyeongae's Salt. What do these different forms of colonial-era displacement share?
3. How does Full House represent the question of "home" — where do the characters belong?
4. Japan has never formally apologized for colonial-era forced migration in the same comprehensive way it has (partially) for comfort women. How does this political context shape the Zainichi literary imagination?
5. Yū Miri later moved to Fukushima, near the DMZ, and has associated herself with North Korea. What does this trajectory suggest about her relationship to Korean identity?
